OTR & RV Installations

Last updated: March 2026
Based on original work by Alan Applegate, K0BG Preserved & expanded for the amateur radio community
In This Article

A Reality Check

Every few months, someone on the forums asks how to "just throw an HF rig" into their Class A motorhome or Peterbilt. The short answer is: you don't just do anything when it comes to OTR trucks and recreational vehicles. The answers aren't always simple, the solutions are almost always more expensive than in a passenger vehicle, and the manufacturers are generally less cooperative—especially when the chassis and coachwork come from different companies, which is nearly always the case.

If you've read the other articles on this site, you know that a successful mobile HF installation depends on a solid ground plane, short cable runs, proper bonding, and effective RFI suppression. OTR trucks and RVs challenge every single one of those requirements. That's not a reason to give up. It's a reason to understand what you're dealing with before you start drilling holes and stringing coax.

🔑 KEY CONCEPT

Nearly everything that makes passenger-vehicle HF installations straightforward—all-steel bodies, short cable runs, a unified ground plane—is absent or compromised in OTR trucks and RVs. Plan accordingly, and budget accordingly.

The Body Problem: It Isn't Steel

This is the single biggest issue and the one that most newcomers fail to grasp. The body of your RV or the sleeper/cab of your OTR truck is, in large part, not steel. Fiberglass, aluminum, composite panels, rubber-mounted cab assemblies—these are the norm, not the exception. A Class A motorhome is essentially a fiberglass box bolted to a chassis. A fifth-wheel is even worse: it has no electrical connection to a tow vehicle's ground plane at all when unhitched.

What does this mean in practice? It means you have no unified, low-impedance ground plane for your antenna system. It means the factory wiring harnesses—which are long, sometimes absurdly so—run through and along non-conducting body panels with only a myriad of ground straps and individual conductors providing any return path. Those long lengths of unshielded wire become antennas in their own right, providing inordinate RF ingress and egress paths.

Every wire in the vehicle becomes a potential source of RFI or a victim of your transmitted signal. The slide-out motors, the leveling jacks, the awning controller, the entertainment system, the LP gas detector, the inverter—all of them are connected by long runs of wire through a body that does absolutely nothing to shield them from RF.

Power Systems: The Good News and the Bad

OTR trucks and RVs are rarely short of electrical power. A typical Class A motorhome has one or two chassis batteries plus a house battery bank, often 400 amp-hours or more. OTR trucks commonly run 150 to 250 amp alternators, and some have dual alternators. You will not have a power problem running a 100-watt HF transceiver.

However—and there's always a however—those large alternators and multiple charging systems are prolific sources of RFI. Dual alternators are particularly troublesome because they can produce intermodulation products that are extremely difficult to filter. The high-current charging cables, which may run six feet or more, radiate broadband noise that loves to couple into your receive system.

⚠ WARNING

Some heavy-duty OTR trucks use 24-volt electrical systems. Check before you connect anything. Most amateur transceivers are designed for 13.8 VDC nominal. Connecting one to a 24-volt system will destroy it instantly, and your warranty won't cover your mistake.

Proper power wiring for the transceiver follows the same principles outlined in the Wiring article, but with the added caveat that your cable runs will almost certainly be longer. Use appropriately sized wire—10 AWG minimum for runs over 10 feet at 100 watts—and fuse the positive lead at the battery. Do not tap into existing circuits, do not use the cigarette lighter socket, and do not rely on the chassis for your negative return. Run a dedicated negative lead — on vehicles without an Intelligent Battery Sensor (IBS), connect directly to the battery negative terminal. On vehicles with an IBS, use a factory ground stud on the engine block. See the Wiring article for details on why this matters.

RFI Suppression: Split Beads Are Not Optional

Let me be blunt: if you're installing HF in an OTR truck or RV, the liberal use of split ferrite beads is mandatory. Not suggested. Not recommended. Mandatory.

The combination of non-conducting body panels, long factory wiring harnesses, and multiple electronic control systems creates an RF environment that would make a passenger-vehicle installer weep. You will need split beads (type 31 or 43 material for HF) on:

  • The transceiver power leads
  • The coax feed line (common mode choke—more on this below)
  • Motor control cables for slides, jacks, and awnings
  • The entertainment system wiring
  • LP gas detector leads
  • Inverter AC and DC leads
  • Any cable that runs more than a few feet through the vehicle

Yes, this gets expensive. Welcome to OTR/RV mobile. Nobody said it was cheap.

Motor Control Cables

The long motor control cable runs in RVs deserve special attention. These cables—going to slide-out motors, leveling jacks, and other actuators—can be 20 feet or more in length. Split beads may not be sufficient. In some cases, you may need to replace the factory cable with shielded cable.

🔑 KEY CONCEPT

If you use shielded cable for motor control runs, ground the shield on the antenna end only—that is, the end closest to your antenna installation. Do NOT ground both ends. Grounding both ends of a shield creates a ground loop that will make your RFI problem worse, not better.

Common Mode Chokes: Non-Negotiable

On any vehicle with non-conducting body surfaces, you must install a common mode choke on the coax feed line to the antenna. This is true whether your antenna is mounted on the roof, on a bumper bracket, or on a trailer hitch mount. The outer surface of the coax braid will carry common mode currents back into the vehicle, and with no conducting body to provide a low-impedance path, those currents will find their way into every wire in the coach.

A proper common mode choke for HF can be made by winding 8 to 10 turns of your coax through a type 31 toroid core (FT-240-31 is the standard choice), or by stacking multiple snap-on split beads of the same material. See the Common Mode Currents article for construction details.

Antenna Mounting: Where the Compromises Live

Antenna mounting on an OTR truck or RV is where most of the painful compromises happen. The ideal HF mobile antenna mount—centered on a large, flat, all-steel roof—simply doesn't exist on these vehicles. You're working with fiberglass roofs, aluminum skin over wooden framing, rubber-mounted cabs, and surfaces that flex and leak if you drill through them carelessly.

⚠ WARNING

Do NOT mount a screwdriver antenna base inside the RV with only the whip protruding through the roof. This is the single lossiest possible configuration. The loading coil must be outside the vehicle where it can radiate. Mounting the base inside puts the high-current portion of the antenna system inside a lossy enclosure surrounded by wiring that will absorb your signal. You will be heard by nobody.

Practical mounting options for RVs include:

  • Roof-mounted base on a steel plate: A steel plate (at least 12 by 12 inches, bonded to whatever chassis ground is accessible) provides a local ground plane. Not ideal, but workable.
  • Bumper or hitch mount: Keeps the antenna on the steel frame, which helps with ground plane. Watch for feed line routing.
  • Ladder mount: If the RV has a steel rear ladder bolted to the frame, this can be a decent mounting point.

For OTR trucks, the mirror brackets and cab roof are common mounting points, but remember that rubber-mounted cabs are electrically isolated from the chassis. You'll need a bonding strap from the cab to the frame.

Stationary Operation: Forget the Tripod Fantasy

Here's where I'm going to upset some people. A mobile antenna stuck on a tripod next to your parked RV, with or without a few radials tossed on the ground, is not an efficient antenna. It's a compromise on top of a compromise. The ground losses with a few short radials on dirt or pavement are enormous, and the radiation pattern is mediocre at best.

🔥 MYTH BUSTED

A pair of Hamstick dipoles on a tripod is not a meaningful improvement. Hamsticks are already a severe compromise as mobile antennas; using them as a fixed dipole when you have the space and time to put up a proper antenna is just lazy. Use a real dipole—even a simple wire dipole strung between two trees or a mast and your RV will dramatically outperform any Hamstick arrangement.

If you're going to be stationary for any length of time, put up a proper dipole. A simple wire dipole for 40 meters costs about five dollars in wire and a balun. It will outperform your mobile antenna on a tripod by 10 to 15 dB on receive and nearly as much on transmit. That's the difference between working a station easily and not hearing them at all.

Cowl and Body Variations

One thing that makes OTR and RV installations especially frustrating is the variation between models and model years. The cowl area—where the windshield meets the dashboard and the firewall transitions to the body—varies enormously. What worked on a 2018 Freightliner Cascadia may not work on a 2020. What worked on a Winnebago View may be completely different on a Navion, even though they share a chassis.

Plastic and fiberglass body sections in the cowl area mean that factory wiring harnesses pass through non-shielding material. If you're chasing RFI, you may need to trace harnesses through areas you didn't expect, and you may find that bonding solutions that work on one vehicle are ineffective on another. Take good notes, be methodical, and don't assume anything transfers between vehicles.

A Practical Approach

If I were starting from scratch on an RV or OTR installation, here's the order I'd tackle things:

  1. Power first. Run dedicated, properly fused, properly sized power leads from the battery to the radio location. Get this right before you do anything else.
  2. Antenna mount and feed line. Choose your mounting location, install the mount with proper bonding to the nearest steel, run the coax with a common mode choke.
  3. Receive-only test. Before you ever transmit, listen. Tune across the bands and identify every source of noise. Fix them one at a time with ferrite beads, starting at the radio and working outward.
  4. Transmit test. Key up into a dummy load first. Does anything in the RV go haywire? If so, you have common mode issues to resolve before you put power to the antenna.
  5. On-air test. Now you can try the antenna. Check SWR, make some contacts, and listen for reports of your signal quality.
💡 TIP

Keep a log of every modification, every bead you install, and every RFI source you identify. OTR and RV installations are iterative—you'll be revisiting and refining for weeks or months. Good notes save you from repeating work.

Summary

Installing HF in an OTR truck or RV is absolutely doable, but it requires more effort, more expense, and more patience than a passenger vehicle. The non-conducting body panels, long cable runs, multiple electronic systems, and limited antenna mounting options all conspire against you. The solutions are the same as in any mobile installation—bonding, common mode suppression, proper wiring, and sensible antenna choices—but you'll need more of everything and you'll need to be more methodical about it.

Don't let forum posts or YouTube videos convince you it's a weekend project. Do it right, or don't do it at all. A poorly installed HF system in an RV is worse than no system—it's a source of frustration and RFI that will sour you on mobile operating forever.

The Modern RV: New Hardware, New Noise

Everything above still applies. But the RV that rolls off the lot in 2025 bears little resemblance to one from 2010, and the new hardware brings new problems for the HF operator. Some of it is actually an improvement. Most of it isn't. Here's what you're dealing with now.

Starlink and Satellite Internet

Starlink has become the internet solution of choice for full-time RVers, and from a connectivity standpoint it's remarkable. From an HF standpoint it's a nightmare. The phased array antenna contains its own power supply unit, and that PSU radiates broadband noise that will peg your S-meter at S7 to S9 across the HF spectrum. It isn't subtle. You will hear it the moment you turn the dish on.

The dish itself is only part of the problem. The PoE cable running from the dish to the router is an excellent RF radiator, and the router adds its own contribution to the noise floor. Type 31 split beads on the PoE cable will help, but they won't eliminate the problem entirely.

💡 TIP

Separate the Starlink dish from your HF antenna by as much distance as the roof allows. If you can manage 10 feet or more, do it. The simplest and most effective solution remains: turn Starlink off when operating HF. If that's unacceptable, be prepared to invest serious time and money in suppression—and accept that you may not get the noise floor all the way down.

Lithium House Batteries

LiFePO4 batteries have become standard equipment in mid-range and higher RVs, and from a pure power standpoint they're a genuine improvement. The voltage stays at a rock-solid 13.2V under load instead of sagging like lead-acid. Your transceiver will appreciate the stable supply. That's the good news.

The bad news is the Battery Management System. Every lithium pack has a BMS board, and that board uses switching circuitry that generates noise. Some BMS designs are quieter than others—this is worth researching and comparing before you buy, because you're not going to swap it out later without considerable effort.

⚠ WARNING

The ugly surprise with lithium batteries: the BMS can and will disconnect the pack mid-transmit with zero warning if it decides the discharge rate, temperature, or cell balance is outside its parameters. One moment you're in a QSO, the next moment your radio is dead. There is no graceful degradation—it's a hard cutoff. Know your BMS limits and stay inside them.

Solar, MPPT Controllers, and Inverters

A modern RV with a solar installation gives you three independent broadband noise sources, and they're all on the roof right next to your antenna.

The solar panels themselves are electrically benign—they're just producing DC. But the wiring between the panels and the charge controller is a coupling path for noise, and it's often run parallel to other wiring in the most convenient (and worst, from an RF perspective) routing.

The MPPT charge controller is the primary offender. It operates at 50 to 150 kHz with switching harmonics that extend well into the HF range. Inverters are worse—a 2000-watt inverter is a broadband noise generator that will obliterate your receive capability on multiple bands simultaneously.

🔑 KEY CONCEPT

Install type 31 ferrite on every lead entering and leaving both the inverter and the MPPT controller—DC input, DC output, AC output, sense wires, all of it. Route solar DC wiring away from coax and antenna feed lines. Physical separation and ferrite together. Neither one alone is sufficient.

Digital Coach Control Systems

This is where modern RVs get genuinely difficult for the HF operator. Systems like Firefly, CZone, and the RV-C protocol use CAN bus running at 250 kbps distributed throughout the entire coach. Every light, vent, slide, and awning is controlled by networked modules, and many of those modules include PWM LED dimmers.

PWM dimmers are HF noise generators. That's not an opinion; it's physics. The switching edges produce harmonics across the HF spectrum, and because the architecture is distributed—modules everywhere, connected by long bus wiring—you can't just clamp a bead on one cable and call it done. The noise sources are spread throughout the vehicle.

Troubleshooting these systems is a longer process than anything else in this article. You'll need to identify which individual modules are the worst offenders by turning circuits on and off methodically while monitoring your receiver. There is no shortcut. Budget your time accordingly.

DEF Systems

If your RV has a diesel chassis built after 2010, it has a Diesel Exhaust Fluid system, and that system is going to make noise. The DEF pump and the PWM heaters for the tank and fluid lines draw 10 to 20 amps and produce broadband noise while operating. The heaters cycle on and off based on temperature, so the noise comes and goes unpredictably.

Here's the rub: you can't disable the DEF system without generating fault codes that will eventually derate the engine or put it in limp mode. Don't even think about it. Put type 31 ferrite on the DEF harness leads, accept whatever improvement you get, and move on. This is one you live with.

Class B Vans

The Sprinter, Transit, and ProMaster conversion vans deserve separate discussion because they're fundamentally different from larger RVs—and in most ways, they're better for HF.

Steel bodies. That's the headline. A Class B van has an all-steel unibody or body-on-frame construction, which means you have a real ground plane. Compared to the fiberglass-and-aluminum construction of a Class A or fifth-wheel, this is an enormous advantage. By a wide margin, Class B vans are the best RV platform for HF mobile.

The tradeoff is space. The compact interior means cable separation between your coax and the vehicle's wiring harnesses is measured in inches, not feet. Roof space is at a premium—you're competing with the Maxxfan, solar panels, Starlink dish, and your HF antenna for a finite number of square feet.

💡 TIP

Plan your roof layout before anything gets installed. Once the Maxxfan is cut in, the solar panels are bonded down, and the Starlink mount is drilled, you're living with those positions. Decide where your HF antenna goes first, then arrange everything else around it. Getting this wrong means a permanent compromise you'll regret every time you tune across a noisy band.

Electric RVs

Thor's Vision Vehicle and the Winnebago eRV represent the leading edge of electric recreational vehicles. For HF operators, they represent uncharted territory.

The traction inverter in an electric drivetrain is a massive switching power converter—we're talking hundreds of kilowatts. The noise it generates will almost certainly make HF operation while driving impractical. This isn't a ferrite-and-move-on situation; the power levels involved are orders of magnitude beyond anything a snap-on bead can address.

Stationary operation is more feasible. With the traction inverter idle, the remaining systems (DC-DC converters, climate control, onboard charger) are comparable to what you'd find in any modern RV, and the same suppression techniques apply. But these are early days. There isn't enough field experience yet to give definitive answers, and anyone who claims otherwise is guessing. If you're an early adopter, take good notes and share what you learn.
