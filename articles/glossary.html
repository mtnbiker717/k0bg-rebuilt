Glossary

Last updated: March 2026
Based on original work by Alan Applegate, K0BG Preserved & expanded for the amateur radio community
In This Article

Mobile amateur radio straddles two technical worlds: RF engineering and automotive electronics. The jargon from both fields can be impenetrable if you are new to either one. This glossary covers the terms you will encounter throughout this site and in the broader mobile HF community. If a term is used on these pages and is not defined here, let us know and we will add it.

💡 TIP

Terms that appear frequently in mobile HF discussions are cross-referenced to the relevant articles on this site where you can find detailed explanations and practical guidance.

A

ABP (Absolute Barometric Pressure)
Also called a MAP (Manifold Absolute Pressure) sensor. One of several pollution control sensors used by the electronic engine control system. Relevant to mobile installations because RF ingress into the engine control wiring can cause false readings from these sensors, triggering check engine lights or erratic engine behavior.
AGM (Absorbent Glass Mat)
A type of sealed lead-acid battery with little or no outgassing. Vibration resistant and typically offers twice the service life of a standard SLI battery. AGM batteries come in SLI, reserve capacity (often mislabeled "deep cycle"), and marine configurations. Sometimes referred to by the Optima brand names RedTop (SLI), YellowTop (reserve capacity), and BlueTop (marine/deep cycle). Exide also makes comparable AGM batteries. If a secondary battery is mounted inside the vehicle, it must be AGM or LiFePO4 to prevent outgassing, which can be explosive.
ARES
Amateur Radio Emergency Service. An adjunct of the ARRL whose purpose is to provide secondary response teams to aid communications in emergency situations.
ATC/ATO
International standard automotive blade fuses patented by Littelfuse in 1976. Amperage ratings from 1 to 40. The ATC style is moisture-sealed; the ATO is not. These are the standard blade fuses found in most vehicle fuse blocks. Not recommended as the primary fuse for high-current amateur radio installations — use Maxi fuses for loads over 30 amps.
ATV
Amateur fast-scan television. Due in part to smaller cameras, mobile ATV has become more popular, especially with ARES and other emergency teams.
Auto-Coupler
Often called an antenna tuner. Provides a conjugate impedance match between the antenna and the transceiver (usually 50 ohms). An auto-coupler works by presenting a 50-ohm load to the transceiver regardless of the actual antenna impedance, but does not eliminate reactance at the antenna feedpoint. Note: RF voltages inside an auto-coupler can exceed 10,000 volts at 100 watts when working a highly reactive load. Do not touch during transmission.
ADAS
Advanced Driver Assistance Systems. Umbrella term for radar cruise control, lane-keeping assist, automatic emergency braking, blind-spot monitoring, and similar features. Uses 77 GHz radar, cameras, and ultrasonics. ADAS wiring and sensors must not be disturbed during radio installation. These are safety-critical systems, and tampering with them can disable features designed to prevent collisions.
APRS
Automatic Packet Reporting System. A digital communications system using the AX.25 protocol, primarily on VHF (144.39 MHz in North America, 144.80 MHz in Europe). APRS is used for real-time position reporting, weather station data, short messaging, and telemetry. Despite the name, the "A" originally stood for "Automatic" rather than a reference to any specific technology. Most modern implementations use GPS for position data, transmitted at regular intervals or triggered by course changes.

B

Ballmount
A type of antenna mount with a built-in adjustable swivel allowing an antenna to be mounted on a surface that is not exactly horizontal or vertical. Popular for trunk lips and hitch receivers. Provides less consistent ground plane contact than a direct through-body mount.
Base Loading
Refers to placing the loading coil at the base of the antenna rather than in the center. Base-loaded designs are simpler and less expensive but provide approximately half the radiation resistance of a center-loaded design of equal physical size, resulting in lower efficiency. See Center Loading.
BJB / BMFB
Battery Junction Box / Battery-Mounted Fuse Block. A consolidated fuse and relay center mounted on or adjacent to the battery positive terminal, found on most modern vehicles (Ford, GM, Stellantis, roughly 2015 and newer). Contains mega fuses protecting main vehicle circuits. A BJB does NOT protect your accessory wiring. You still need your own inline fuse on the radio power cable, sized and located per the Wiring article.
B-CAN
Body Controller Area Network. A vehicle data bus that connects body electronics including lighting, power locks, windows, mirrors, and seat controls. B-CAN operates at varying data rates (typically 125 to 250 kbps depending on the manufacturer) and can be a significant source of RFI, producing evenly-spaced birdies across the HF spectrum. The spacing of these birdies corresponds to the bus clock frequency. See the RFI Problems article for mitigation strategies.
BMS
Battery Management System. The electronic circuitry that monitors and protects lithium-based batteries (including LiFePO4). A BMS controls charging voltage and current, prevents over-discharge, monitors cell temperatures, and performs cell balancing to keep all cells in a pack at equal state of charge. A quality BMS is not optional with lithium batteries; it is a safety requirement. Cheap units with inadequate protection have caused vehicle fires.
Bonding
The practice of installing low-impedance grounding straps between the vehicle frame or unibody and all bolted-on components (hood, trunk lid, doors, fenders, exhaust system) to ensure RF continuity across the entire vehicle surface. Bonding is NOT primarily about DC safety grounding; it is about making the vehicle body function as a coherent ground plane and counterpoise for the antenna system. Poor bonding is the single most common cause of RFI and common-mode problems in mobile installations. See the Bonding article.

C

Cap Hat
A capacitive hat (disk, spokes, or ring) placed at the top of an antenna whip to increase the effective capacitance of the element above the loading coil. This raises the current node and increases radiation efficiency without physically lengthening the antenna. Cap hats are particularly effective for improving performance on the lower HF bands where the antenna is most severely shortened relative to a full-size quarter wave.
Center Loading
Refers to having the loading coil at approximately the midpoint of the antenna rather than at the base. Center loading provides approximately twice the radiation resistance of base loading but requires about twice the inductive reactance to achieve resonance. The higher radiation resistance means better efficiency and improved matching at the feedpoint. Most quality mobile HF antennas use center or above-center coil placement for this reason.
Corona Ball
A round metal ball placed at the top of the antenna whip to minimize static discharge. Acts as a corona suppressor by distributing the electric field over a larger area, reducing the field gradient that causes corona discharge noise. As a general rule, anything smaller than 1/2 inch in diameter is insufficient, especially if the retaining screw enters from the side rather than the top. Static drains work in conjunction with corona balls. Particularly valuable in dry, dusty climates where static buildup is severe.
Common Mode
Current flowing on the outside of a coaxial cable shield, or equally (in phase) on both conductors of a balanced line. Common mode current is unwanted in virtually all amateur radio installations. In mobile work, the level of common mode current on the feed line is directly related to the quality of the vehicle's ground plane. High common mode current causes RFI to vehicle electronics, distorted audio reports, and RF burns. See the Common Mode Currents article.
Counterpoise
A conductor or system of conductors used as a substitute for a direct earth ground connection, forming the "other half" of the antenna system. In mobile installations, the vehicle body serves as the counterpoise. Its effectiveness depends entirely on bonding quality and the antenna mounting location relative to the vehicle's metal surface area. A vehicle with poor bonding is a poor counterpoise, regardless of how expensive the antenna is.

D

D-STAR
Digital Smart Technologies for Amateur Radio. A digital voice protocol developed by JARL (Japan Amateur Radio League), implemented primarily by Icom. Uses the AMBE vocoder for voice compression. The internet gateway system links D-STAR repeaters worldwide, allowing global communication through local repeater access.
DMR
Digital Mobile Radio. An ETSI TDMA digital voice standard adopted by the amateur radio community. Provides two time slots per 12.5 kHz channel, effectively doubling repeater capacity. The Brandmeister and TGIF talkgroup networks provide worldwide connectivity via internet-linked repeaters.

E

EPS
Electronic Power Steering. Motor-assisted steering that has replaced hydraulic systems in virtually all vehicles since roughly 2015. The PWM motor controller can generate broadband HF noise during steering input, particularly noticeable on the lower bands. EPS is safety-critical. If RF from your transmitter causes anomalous steering behavior, stop transmitting immediately and investigate before operating again.
ERP
Effective Radiated Power. The actual power radiated by an antenna system in its direction of maximum gain, calculated as the product of transmitter output power, feed line efficiency, and antenna gain (or loss, as is usually the case with mobile HF antennas). ERP is the number that actually determines whether your signal is heard at the other end. A 100-watt transmitter feeding a mobile antenna with 6 dB of loss produces an ERP of 25 watts. Understanding this keeps your expectations realistic.

F

Ferrite
A mixture of iron oxides and rare metals used to make baluns, chokes, and UNUNs. Comes in various shapes (toroids, split beads, rods, sleeves), sizes, and permeability values called "mixes." Different mixes are optimized for different frequency ranges. Mix 31 is ideal for HF motor lead chokes and common mode suppression. Mix 43 is useful across a broader frequency range. Do not substitute mixes — the wrong mix will not provide adequate impedance at the frequencies where you need it.
FT8 / FT4
Weak-signal digital modes developed by K1JT and implemented in WSJT-X software. FT8 uses 15-second transmit/receive cycles and can decode signals down to -21 dB SNR. Extraordinarily effective for mobile HF where antenna efficiency is poor and signals that would be inaudible on SSB are decoded reliably. Requires a computer, sound card interface, and accurate time synchronization. Operate only while parked.
Fusion
Yaesu System Fusion. A C4FM digital voice protocol for VHF and UHF. Backward compatible with analog FM, so a Fusion-equipped radio works on any analog repeater. Wires-X provides internet linking between Fusion repeaters. Built into current Yaesu mobile and handheld transceivers.

G

GaN
Gallium Nitride. A wide-bandgap semiconductor material used in RF power amplifiers. Offers higher power density and efficiency than silicon LDMOS at RF frequencies. GaN devices are entering the amateur market in some commercial amplifier designs but are not yet common in mobile HF amplifiers.
Ground Loop
A condition where multiple ground paths between two pieces of equipment create a closed loop that can act as a receiving antenna, picking up noise, RFI, and alternator whine. Ground loops are a frequent problem in mobile installations where the transceiver, amplifier, and antenna controller each have their own ground connections to different points on the vehicle. The solution is a single-point grounding scheme, not adding more ground wires. See the Wiring article.

I

IBS
Intelligent Battery Sensor. A sensor mounted on the negative battery cable that monitors current, voltage, and temperature, reporting to the vehicle's power management system. Present on most vehicles from roughly 2015 onward. If you bypass the IBS by connecting your radio's negative lead to the battery terminal above the sensor, the vehicle loses track of current draw, sets diagnostic trouble codes, and may exhibit erratic charging behavior. On IBS-equipped vehicles, connect the radio negative lead to a factory chassis ground stud instead.
IMD
Intermodulation Distortion. Spurious signals generated when two or more signals mix in a non-linear device such as a transistor amplifier stage. In mobile HF context, the most relevant fact is that solid-state transceivers generate excessive IMD when presented with an SWR much above 1.8:1. The output transistors are forced into non-linear operation by the reflected power, producing splatter on adjacent frequencies. This is why proper antenna matching matters: not just for your own signal quality, but out of courtesy to others on the band.

J

Jump Points
Pre-stored antenna motor positions in a turn-counting controller, each corresponding to the resonant point on a specific band. When you press the button for 20 meters, the controller drives the motor to the stored position for that band. Properly calibrated jump points let you change bands in seconds rather than minutes. See the Antenna Controllers and Coil Adjustment articles for setup procedures.

L

LDT
Live Data Terminal. A vehicle diagnostic interface used by dealership technicians to read real-time sensor data, perform module programming, and clear fault codes. Relevant to mobile installations because aftermarket wiring that interferes with vehicle data buses can set diagnostic trouble codes that only an LDT can properly clear.
LFP / LiFePO4
Lithium Iron Phosphate. A lithium battery chemistry increasingly popular for auxiliary power in mobile amateur radio installations. LiFePO4 cells have a nominal voltage of 3.2V (four cells in series produce 12.8V, a near-perfect match for 12V systems). They offer high energy density, long cycle life (2000+ cycles), and excellent thermal stability compared to other lithium chemistries. They require a BMS and have a different charging profile than SLI lead-acid batteries: the alternator's charge voltage must be regulated or the BMS will disconnect to protect the cells.
LID
Amateur radio slang for a poor or inconsiderate operator. Apocryphally said to stand for "Licensed Idiot Designation," though the term predates that backronym. You will encounter LIDs on every band. Do not be one.
Loading Coil
An inductor placed in series with an antenna element to electrically lengthen a physically short antenna. The coil adds the inductive reactance needed to cancel the capacitive reactance of the short element, bringing the antenna to resonance. In screwdriver antennas, a motor-driven variable loading coil allows tuning across multiple bands. The trade-off is that energy stored in the coil's magnetic field is subject to resistive loss, reducing radiation efficiency. See the Coil Adjustment article.
LRC
Load Response Control. Smart alternator systems that vary output voltage between roughly 12.4V and 15.5V based on battery state of charge, engine load, and fuel economy targets. Found on most vehicles since approximately 2016. The old assumption that your vehicle electrical system sits at a steady 13.8V is outdated. LRC systems can drop voltage below 13V during cruise conditions and spike above 15V during regenerative braking, which affects transceiver behavior and auxiliary battery charging.

M

M17
An open-source digital voice protocol using the Codec2 vocoder, which is entirely patent-free. Still in early-stage deployment with limited hardware support. Worth watching as a potential alternative to the proprietary D-STAR, DMR, and Fusion ecosystems.
Mag Mount
Magnetic mount antenna base. Uses one or more permanent magnets to temporarily attach an antenna to a vehicle's roof, trunk, or other flat metal surface. Adequate for VHF and UHF work where the short wavelength makes ground plane quality less critical. On HF, however, mag mounts introduce significant loss because the RF ground connection depends on capacitive coupling through the paint and the small contact area of the magnets. For serious HF mobile work, a permanent through-body mount is strongly preferred. See the Installation Notes article.
Matching Coil
A small inductor, typically a shunt coil, installed at the antenna feed point to transform the antenna's impedance to match the 50-ohm feed line. On mobile HF antennas where the radiation resistance is well below 50 ohms, a matching coil (sometimes called a shunt matching network) provides a simple, broadband impedance transformation. Also provides a DC ground path for the antenna element, which helps dissipate static charge. See the Antenna Matching article.
Maxifuse
A blade-type automotive fuse rated from 20A to 80A, physically larger than standard ATC/ATO fuses. Commonly used to protect the main power feed from the battery to radio equipment. Maxifuses are available at any auto parts store and fit standard Maxifuse holders. Always fuse both the positive and negative leads at the battery end of the power cable.
MICS
Media Independent Communication System. A vehicle data bus protocol used by some manufacturers for communication between electronic modules. Like B-CAN, MICS bus activity can generate RFI on HF frequencies. The exact characteristics depend on the implementation and data rate.
MIL
Malfunction Indicator Lamp. The "check engine" light on your dashboard. In the context of mobile radio installation, a lit MIL can be triggered by aftermarket wiring that interferes with vehicle electronic modules, damaged wiring harnesses from improper antenna mount installation, or RFI from the transmitter coupling into vehicle sensor circuits. If your MIL illuminates after a radio installation, the two events are probably related.

N

NanoVNA
A low-cost handheld vector network analyzer that measures SWR, impedance (R+jX), return loss, and displays Smith charts. Has largely replaced dedicated antenna analyzers for many amateur applications. Invaluable for mobile antenna adjustment and choke verification. Companion software on a laptop provides a larger display and data logging when the small screen proves insufficient in the field.
NMO
New Motorola mount. The industry-standard professional mobile antenna mounting system, requiring a 3/4-inch (19mm) hole through the vehicle surface. NMO mounts provide excellent RF grounding, mechanical strength, and weather sealing when properly installed. Genuine NMO mounts from reputable manufacturers (Larsen, PCTEL, Laird) are precision-machined and meet tight tolerances. Be aware that cheap import "NMO-style" mounts are often not dimensionally compatible with genuine NMO connectors and antennas, leading to intermittent connections and water intrusion.

O

OBDII
On-Board Diagnostics, version II. The standardized vehicle diagnostic port mandated on all vehicles sold in the United States since 1996. The OBDII connector is required to be located within 3 feet of the driver's seating position, typically under the dashboard on the driver's side. The port provides access to the vehicle's diagnostic data bus and is used with scan tools to read fault codes. Relevant to mobile radio work because it is the interface through which RFI-induced fault codes can be read and cleared.
OTA
Over-the-Air updates. Wireless software updates pushed to vehicle ECUs, now standard on most manufacturers. OTA updates can change power management behavior, CAN bus timing, or module firmware without warning. A vehicle that was RFI-clean yesterday can develop new noise sources overnight after an OTA update. If new interference appears with no changes to your radio installation, check whether the vehicle recently received a software update.

P

Packet Radio
A computer-to-computer communications mode where data is sent in small packets containing addressing and error-correcting codes. The packet protocol standard used in amateur radio is AX.25. Requires a TNC to interface between the radio and computer. Largely superseded by modern digital modes for long-distance communication, but still used for APRS and some emergency communications applications.
PEP (Peak Envelope Power)
The average power supplied to the antenna transmission line during one RF cycle at the crest of the modulation envelope. PEP is the standard measure of transmitter output power for SSB and other modes with varying amplitude. Most radio specifications and license class power limits are expressed in PEP. Average power is considerably lower — on SSB voice, average power is roughly one-quarter of PEP for typical speech patterns.
Polyfuse
A resettable fuse based on a PTC (Positive Temperature Coefficient) thermistor. When overcurrent heats the device, its resistance increases dramatically, effectively opening the circuit. When the device cools, it resets to its low-resistance state. Polyfuses are used in some antenna controllers and transceivers for overcurrent protection. A notable gotcha: in cold weather, motor running current increases (cold lubricants are stiffer), which can cause a polyfuse to trip prematurely. If your antenna motor stops running in winter but works fine in summer, a polyfuse is likely the culprit.
POTA / SOTA
Parks on the Air / Summits on the Air. Organized programs that award credits for making contacts from designated parks or mountain summits. POTA in particular has driven enormous growth in mobile and portable HF activity since roughly 2020. Many POTA activators operate mobile from their vehicles in park parking areas, making proper mobile HF installation directly relevant.

Q

QRO
Refers to high transmit power, generally 100 watts or more. From the Q code meaning "increase transmitter power."
QRP
Refers to low transmit power, generally 5 watts or less. From the Q code meaning "reduce transmitter power." QRP mobile operation is a growing activity, especially paired with compact amplifiers for a "QRP rig plus small amplifier" approach that keeps current draws manageable.
QSL (misuse on phone)
Q signal universally misused on phone transmissions. QSL properly means "I confirm receipt of your transmission." On phone, "roger" or a simple acknowledgment is correct. Repeated use of "QSL" on phone is a sign of poor operating practice.

R

RFI (Radio Frequency Interference)
In this context, primary sources are ignition and injection systems, electronic engine controls, rolling and rain static, and the brushes of the various electric motors incorporated into vehicles. Amateur radio equipment can both generate and receive RFI. The goal is to eliminate or reduce interference to an acceptable level using proper wiring, bonding, ferrite suppression, and antenna placement. See the RFI Problems article.
Radiation Resistance
The component of an antenna's feed point impedance that represents actual power radiated as electromagnetic waves. Radiation resistance is directly proportional to the square of the antenna's electrical length. A full-size quarter-wave vertical has a radiation resistance of about 36 ohms. A typical mobile HF antenna on 40 meters might have a radiation resistance of only 2 to 5 ohms, meaning that the vast majority of the feed point impedance is loss resistance in the loading coil and ground system. This is why every fraction of an ohm matters in mobile HF, and why proper bonding and the longest possible whip are so important.
Reactance
The imaginary component of impedance, caused by inductance (positive reactance, +jX) or capacitance (negative reactance, -jX), measured in ohms. At resonance, an antenna's inductive and capacitive reactances cancel and the net reactance equals zero. Reactance causes energy to be stored and returned to the source rather than radiated, which is why tuning an antenna to resonance (zero reactance) matters. Your antenna analyzer's reactance reading is the single most important number during antenna adjustment.
Reed Switch
A magnetically-actuated switch consisting of two ferromagnetic contacts sealed in a glass envelope. In turn-counting antenna controllers, a small magnet attached to the motor shaft passes a reed switch once per revolution. The controller counts these pulses to track the coil position. Reed switches have a finite life (typically rated for millions of cycles) and can fail, causing the controller to lose track of position. A failed reed switch is a common cause of a controller that "overshoots" stored positions.
Repeater
An automated radio station, usually located on a hilltop or tall structure, that receives signals on one frequency (the input) and simultaneously retransmits them on another frequency (the output), extending the communication range of low-power mobile and handheld stations. Repeaters are primarily a VHF/UHF resource and are largely outside the scope of this site's focus on HF mobile operation, but many mobile operators maintain VHF/UHF capability for local communication alongside their HF installation.
Resonance
The frequency at which an antenna's net reactance equals zero; that is, where the inductive and capacitive reactances exactly cancel. At resonance, the feed point impedance is purely resistive. Critically, resonance is NOT necessarily the point of lowest SWR. On mobile HF antennas where the radiation resistance is far from 50 ohms, the lowest SWR point and the resonant frequency can differ by tens of kilohertz. Always tune for zero reactance (resonance) first, then address the resistive mismatch separately. See the Antenna Matching and Coil Adjustment articles.

S

Remote Tuned Antenna
Includes the Scorpion, all motorized screwdriver antennas, and some base-loaded antennas where the resonant point is adjusted by a remotely controlled motor. Requires properly choked motor leads (placed outside the vehicle at the antenna base), a suitable controller, and correct impedance matching. See the Antenna Controllers article.
Reserve Capacity Battery
A type of battery designed to be discharged at a slower rate than an SLI battery. May be used in an SLI starting application but must be sized larger because its maximum cold cranking amperage is lower than a standard SLI. Sometimes referred to as a YellowTop, a trademark of the Optima battery company. Often mislabeled "deep cycle" — but like any lead-acid battery, it should not be discharged below 10.5 volts under load.
Screwdriver (antenna)
A continuously-variable HF mobile antenna that uses a DC motor to drive a shorting contact up and down a loading coil, changing the inductance in the circuit and thereby tuning the antenna across multiple bands. The name originates from the earliest designs, which repurposed the motor and gear train from a battery-powered cordless screwdriver. Modern screwdriver antennas use purpose-built motors but the name persists. They are the most versatile type of mobile HF antenna, capable of covering 3.5 to 54 MHz with a single antenna, at the cost of mechanical complexity.
Shadowing
Signal attenuation caused by the vehicle body partially blocking the antenna's radiation pattern. A roof-mounted antenna is shadowed in the direction of the vehicle body below it; a trunk-lip mount is shadowed by the entire vehicle in the forward direction. Shadowing can amount to 6 dB or more in the blocked direction, which is a full S-unit. Mount location selection is a trade-off between shadowing, ground plane quality, and mechanical constraints. See the Installation Notes article.
Simplex
Direct radio-to-radio communication on a single frequency, without the use of a repeater. All HF communication is simplex (or split-frequency simplex in the case of cross-band DX contacts). On VHF/UHF, simplex operation is used for direct local communication when repeater access is not needed or not available.
SLI Battery
Starting, Lighting, Ignition. The standard lead-acid battery found in virtually all motor vehicles. SLI batteries are designed to deliver very high current for short periods (engine cranking) and to be maintained near full charge by the alternator. They are NOT designed for deep discharge cycles. Drawing an SLI battery below 50% state of charge significantly shortens its life. If you need deep-cycle capability for extended radio operation with the engine off, install a separate auxiliary battery (preferably LiFePO4).
SOC
State of Charge. The remaining usable capacity of a battery expressed as a percentage, where 100% is fully charged and 0% is fully discharged. For SLI lead-acid batteries, useful SOC range is roughly 50% to 100%. For LiFePO4, the usable range is typically 10% to 100%, which is one of their significant advantages for auxiliary power applications.
Split Beads
Ferrite split beads come in various sizes and ferrite mixes. They are called "split beads" because they are divided along their length, allowing them to be snapped over existing wiring without splicing. Usually enclosed in a snap-on plastic cover. When properly applied in sufficient quantity, they act as chokes to reduce or eliminate RFI ingress and egress. Have little effect on audio-frequency interference (AFI). For HF motor lead chokes, Mix 31 in the 3/4-inch ID size is specified. Do not assume a single bead provides meaningful suppression — multiple turns are required for adequate impedance.
SRS
Supplemental Restraint System. The vehicle's airbag system, including crash sensors, the airbag control module, wiring harnesses, and the inflator units located in the steering wheel, dashboard, door panels, headliner, and seat bolsters. SRS components are EXTREMELY dangerous to work around. Airbag inflators contain explosive charges that can cause severe injury or death if accidentally triggered. Never run radio wiring near SRS harnesses (typically identified by yellow connectors and conduit). If you must remove interior panels that contain SRS components, disconnect the battery and wait a minimum of 10 minutes for the backup capacitors to discharge before proceeding. Better yet, do not remove those panels at all. See the Safety article.
Start-Stop
Automatic engine start-stop systems found on most modern vehicles. The engine shuts off at stoplights to save fuel and restarts automatically when the brake is released. During the off period, the alternator produces no output. The starter restart causes a voltage transient that can dip to 9 or 10 volts, which is enough to reset transceivers, corrupt controller memory, and drop digital mode connections. Disable start-stop during HF operation if your vehicle provides that option.
Strapping
The process of installing bonding straps between vehicle body panels and the frame or unibody structure to ensure RF continuity. Often used interchangeably with "bonding," though strapping specifically refers to the physical act of connecting panels with flat copper braid. See the Bonding article for complete guidance on materials, methods, and what to strap.
SWR
Standing Wave Ratio. A dimensionless measure of the impedance mismatch between a transmission line and its load (the antenna). An SWR of 1:1 indicates a perfect match; higher ratios indicate increasing mismatch. Modern solid-state transceivers begin to reduce output power at SWR levels above approximately 2:1 to protect their output transistors, and most will shut down entirely above 3:1. SWR is the number most operators obsess over, but by itself it tells you nothing about whether the mismatch is resistive or reactive. See the Antenna Matching article for why this distinction matters.

T

Telemetrics
Remote monitoring and management systems, commonly used in commercial vehicles for fleet tracking, driver behavior monitoring, fuel management, and maintenance scheduling. Telemetrics systems typically include a cellular modem, GPS receiver, and connections to the vehicle's data bus. They can be a source of RFI due to their always-on cellular transmitters and data bus polling activity. In commercial vehicle installations (see OTR & RV), telemetrics interference must be identified and addressed.
TNC
Terminal Node Controller. A device (hardware or software-implemented) that converts digital data to and from audio tones for transmission and reception via a radio. TNCs implement the AX.25 protocol used by packet radio and APRS. Modern implementations are often software-based (software TNCs or software modems), eliminating the need for dedicated hardware. A TNC is required for APRS operation unless using a radio with a built-in TNC such as the Kenwood TM-D710.
TPS
Throttle Position Sensor. An electronic sensor in the vehicle's engine management system that reports the position of the throttle plate (or the electronic throttle actuator in drive-by-wire systems) to the engine control module. Relevant to mobile radio installations because TPS signal wiring, if routed near radio power cables, can pick up RFI and cause erratic engine behavior during transmission. Maintain adequate separation between radio wiring and engine management harnesses.

V

VOX (Voice Operated Transmit)
A circuit that automatically keys the transmitter when audio is detected at the microphone input. Commonly used for base station hands-free operation. Its use for mobile operation is strongly discouraged: the high background noise level in a vehicle (wind, road noise, HVAC) will cause the VOX to trigger randomly. The predictable result is broadcasting your driving commentary and road rage to everyone on the repeater.
Whip
The antenna section above the loading coil. Also called a stinger. Lengths vary between a few inches and several feet depending on the antenna design and operating band. Longer whips raise the current node higher on the antenna, increasing radiation resistance and efficiency — especially on the lower HF bands. All else being equal, use the longest whip that is mechanically practical for your situation.

U

UNUN
An impedance transformer that matches an unbalanced source to an unbalanced load (hence UN-UN, as opposed to a BAL-UN which matches balanced to unbalanced). In mobile HF antenna systems, a UNUN is used to transform the low feed point impedance of the antenna (often 12 to 25 ohms on the mid-HF bands) up to the 50-ohm impedance of the coaxial feed line. Typical ratios are 4:1 or 9:1. A properly designed UNUN also provides a DC ground path for the antenna element, which is important for dissipating static charges that would otherwise damage the transceiver's front end.
