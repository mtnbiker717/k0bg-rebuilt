ABCs of Mobile HF

Last updated: March 2026
Based on original work by Alan Applegate, K0BG Preserved & expanded for the amateur radio community
In This Article

Mobile HF is one of the most rewarding aspects of amateur radio. It is also one of the most misunderstood, most poorly executed, and most frustrating endeavors you will ever undertake — if you do it wrong. And the overwhelming majority of hams do it wrong, usually because they were in a hurry, listened to bad advice on a forum, or both.

This article is your starting point. Read it completely before you buy a single piece of equipment, drill a single hole, or run a single wire. I mean that. If you skip ahead and start bolting things to your vehicle, you will almost certainly end up doing it twice — and the second time will cost more than the first.

Slow Down

The single most important piece of advice I can give you is this: do not get in a hurry. A proper mobile HF installation is not a weekend project. The average install, done correctly, takes well over a day of actual labor. For a first-timer, plan on a full weekend at minimum, and do not be surprised if it stretches into a second.

Getting in a hurry to go mobile is a prescription for spending more money than you intended. You will buy the wrong mount, discover your wiring is inadequate, find out your antenna does not perform as advertised, and end up ripping everything out to start over. I have watched this cycle repeat itself hundreds of times over the decades, and it always begins the same way: someone gets excited, skips the planning, and starts bolting things together.

🔑 KEY CONCEPT

Plan carefully, then follow your plan. A proper mobile installation is a system — antenna, mount, bonding, wiring, and radio all work together. A weakness in any one area degrades the whole system. You cannot fix a bad installation by throwing money at one component.

Know Your Vehicle

Before you do anything else, buy a Service Manual for your specific vehicle. Not a Haynes manual, not a Chilton guide — the actual factory service manual, or at minimum, a high-quality reproduction. You need to know where every wire runs, where every airbag is located, where the fuel lines are routed, and how to remove trim panels without breaking the clips that hold them in place.

It is often necessary to remove seats or trim panels to route wiring properly. If you do not know how the trim panels come apart, you will break the retaining clips, and your interior will rattle for the life of the vehicle. A service manual pays for itself the first time you avoid snapping a $15 clip that the dealer has to order from Japan.

Hybrid and Electric Vehicles

If your installation includes HF coverage and you drive a hybrid or full electric vehicle, change your plans. I am not being flippant. Hybrid and EV powertrains generate staggering amounts of broadband electrical noise across the HF spectrum. The inverters, motor controllers, and high-voltage DC-DC converters produce noise floors that will bury all but the strongest signals. Some owners have managed marginal success on certain bands with extensive filtering and shielding, but the results are almost never satisfactory for serious HF operation.

VHF and UHF operation in hybrids and EVs is generally workable, as the noise is predominantly an HF problem. But if HF is your goal, you need a conventionally powered vehicle.

Aluminum-Bodied Vehicles

Aluminum-bodied vehicles — and there are more of them every year — require special considerations regarding wiring, antenna mounting, and bonding. The primary concern is galvanic corrosion, which occurs when dissimilar metals are in contact in the presence of moisture. Steel bolts in aluminum panels, copper wire against aluminum body panels, and steel antenna mounts on aluminum roofs will all corrode if not properly isolated or treated. This is not a theoretical concern; it is a certainty. Consult the Bonding article for specific guidance.

Where to Mount the Radio

⚠ WARNING

Do not mount anything atop the dashboard. Modern vehicles have passenger-side airbags concealed behind the dashboard surface. When an airbag deploys, it does so with explosive force. Anything sitting on the dash becomes a projectile aimed directly at the occupants. A two-pound transceiver head launched at 200 mph will kill you just as dead as the accident would have.

Do not mount gear in foot wells, especially the driver's. Anything in the driver's foot well can slide under the brake pedal. It does not matter how well you think you secured it. In a panic stop or a collision, things move. If your radio or a mounting bracket ends up under the brake pedal, you cannot stop the vehicle. This is not hypothetical — it has happened.

A popular mounting location for remotable transceivers is under the passenger seat. This can work well, but use extra care. You need to ensure the transceiver body has adequate ventilation (most seats restrict airflow), that the wiring is not pinched by the seat rails, and that the unit is secured firmly enough to survive a collision. Also verify there are no seat-mounted side airbag components in the way. Check your service manual.

Mounting Standards

Let me be blunt about what is and is not acceptable for securing equipment in a vehicle:

No magnets. No bungee cords. No wedged wood. No double-sided tape. No Velcro.

None of these are adequate. A vehicle experiences vibration, temperature extremes, and the occasional sudden deceleration. Anything held in place by friction, adhesive, or hope will eventually come loose, and it will do so at the worst possible moment. Use proper mounting brackets bolted to structural members of the vehicle. If you cannot find a commercial bracket for your vehicle, fabricate one from aluminum plate. There is no shortcut here.

💡 TIP

Be neat and tidy. Dangling wires are a distraction and a safety hazard. Every wire in the vehicle should be routed out of sight, secured with proper cable ties or loom, and protected from chafing against sheet metal edges. If your installation looks messy, it probably is messy — and messy installations develop problems.

Wiring: The Foundation You Cannot Skip

Proper wiring is just as important as any other step in a mobile installation, and arguably more so. Bad wiring causes noise, voltage drops, intermittent operation, blown fuses, and in the worst case, vehicle fires.

⚠ WARNING

NEVER use existing vehicle wiring to power radio gear. The vehicle's electrical system is engineered for the vehicle's needs, not yours. Tapping into existing circuits introduces ground loops, shares noisy circuits with your receiver, and risks overloading wiring that was sized for a specific load. Run dedicated power leads directly from the battery, with proper fusing at the battery end. Both the positive and negative leads must be dedicated. See the Wiring article for complete details.

Antennas: The Hard Truth

This is where most hams go wrong, because this is where marketing meets physics, and physics always wins.

Mounting Method Matters

All antennas on clip mounts, lip mounts, and mag mounts will be lossier than permanently through-hole mounted ones. Period. A mag mount sitting on your roof is not making the same connection to the vehicle body that a properly installed NMO or SO-239 mount makes. The capacitive coupling through the paint and the mag mount's rubber boot introduces loss, and worse, it provides an inadequate RF ground plane. If you are serious about mobile HF, drill the hole. I know, I know — it affects resale value. So does rust, and that mag mount is doing nothing to prevent water from pooling where it sits.

Matching and Efficiency

🔥 MYTH BUSTED

Any antenna that does not require matching will be much lossier than one that does. If your antenna presents a near-perfect 50-ohm match across the entire HF spectrum without a tuner or matching network, it is achieving that match through resistive loss — your transmit power is being converted to heat, not radiated. A truly efficient antenna is reactive and requires a matching device to present 50 ohms to the transceiver.

Overall length matters. Radiation resistance is directly related to the square of the electrical length. A short antenna is inherently less efficient than a tall one. There is no magic design, no secret loading coil configuration, and no wonder material that changes this fundamental relationship. A 3-foot HF whip is a compromise. A 7-foot whip is less of a compromise. A full quarter-wave on 20 meters (about 16.5 feet) is what the physics wants.

All remotely-tuned (motorized) antennas require an adequately-sized motor lead choke — no exceptions. Without it, RF from your transmitter travels down the motor leads, into the controller, and into the vehicle wiring. The result is RFI, erratic controller behavior, and potentially damaged electronics.

⚠ ANTENNA SELECTION — WHAT TO AVOID
  • Don't even think about buying a Yaesu ATAS. It is the lossiest, most trouble-prone, remotely-tuned antenna money can buy.
  • Don't buy an antenna with a coil larger than 3.5 inches in diameter.
  • Don't buy an antenna with a coil smaller than 1.75 inches in diameter.
  • Don't buy an antenna with large metal end caps.
  • Don't buy an antenna which requires an external tuner, or is advertised as "automatic tuning." Antennas advertised as having automatic band switching and/or requiring an external tuner to make them work are nothing short of a scam.
  • Any antenna mounted on clip, lip, and mag mounts will be lossier than permanently (through-hole) mounted ones — and will require an extra-high impedance common mode coax choke, bar none.
  • Avoid models which use a Polyfuse or interrupted thread drive screw to protect the motor, as they are incompatible with all automatic antenna controllers.

Using a trailer hitch mount to avoid drilling a hole will reduce your ERP (Effective Radiated Power) by at least 50%. There is no negotiating with physics.

🔥 MYTH BUSTED

The ability to work any given station with any given antenna is NOT indicative of antenna efficiency. Propagation, the other station's equipment, and sheer luck play enormous roles. The fact that you worked a DX station with your hamstick does not mean the hamstick is efficient. It means conditions were good enough to overcome the antenna's deficiencies. Try that same contact on a marginal day and see what happens.

Automation Is Not Optional

Make everything as automatic as possible. This is not about convenience — it is about safety. You are operating a motor vehicle and a radio station simultaneously. Every moment you spend fiddling with controls is a moment you are not watching the road.

A top priority is an automatic antenna controller. Manually adjusting a screwdriver antenna or swapping resonators while driving is a recipe for an accident. A good controller will retune the antenna in seconds when you change bands, without requiring you to take your eyes off the road or your hands off the wheel. Yes, they cost money. So do funerals. See the Antenna Controllers article for recommendations.

Choosing a Transceiver

Selecting a transceiver for mobile use is a deeply personal decision, but there are some universal guidelines:

Do not trust online reviews. Most online reviews are written by people who just spent $1,500 and need to justify the purchase to themselves. They are not objective assessments. The only review that matters is your own hands-on experience.

Do not buy anything older than 10 years unless you have verified that repair parts are still available. Mobile environments are brutal on electronics — heat, vibration, voltage spikes — and a radio that cannot be repaired is an expensive paperweight.

Do not buy without playing with it first. Visit a dealer, attend a hamfest, borrow one from a club member. You need to operate the controls, see the display in sunlight, and determine whether the ergonomics work for you in a vehicle environment. What works beautifully on a desk may be impossible to operate while driving.

Remotable transceivers (where the control head separates from the body) are strongly preferred for mobile use. They give you far more flexibility in mounting locations and keep the heavy, heat-generating main body out of your limited cockpit space.

🔑 TRANSCEIVER CHECKLIST

Before you buy any mobile transceiver, run through this list:

  • Don't put any faith in on-line reviews.
  • Don't consider any transceiver older than 10 years, unless you know repair parts are available and you can do the job yourself.
  • Don't buy any transceiver you can't play with first. This eliminates auction sites as a source.
  • Don't buy any transceiver that has been modified in any way, shape, or form.
  • Be very leery of bundled deals, particularly transceiver/antenna combinations.
  • Built-in auto-couplers are not a basic requirement for any mobile transceiver.
  • Buy a transceiver which is remotable — the reasons should be obvious.

Transceivers with IF-based DSP (Icom IC-7000, IC-7100, IC-7300, and Yaesu FT-991) are preferred over AF-based DSP units for mobile operation. IF-based DSPs have adjustable bandwidths and generally do not require additional IF filters for adequate selectivity. In a mobile scenario, selectivity is much more important than sensitivity.

The Bottom Line

A successful mobile HF installation is the product of careful planning, quality components, proper technique, and patience. There are no shortcuts. Every corner you cut will manifest as a problem later — noise you cannot track down, intermittent connections, poor antenna performance, or an installation that simply looks and feels amateur in the worst sense of the word.

Read through the other articles on this site before you start. Understand bonding, understand wiring, understand antenna fundamentals. Then make your plan. Then follow your plan. You will end up with an installation that works well, looks professional, and lasts for years.

Or you can skip all of this, slap a mag mount on the roof and a cigarette lighter power cord on the radio, and spend the next two years wondering why mobile HF does not work. Your choice.

What Has Changed Since This Was Written

The fundamentals above have not changed — physics does not care what year it is. But the vehicle landscape and the operating landscape have shifted considerably, and a few topics deserve updated treatment.

Hybrid and EV Vehicles: A More Nuanced Picture

The original advice to avoid hybrids and EVs for HF was correct in its day and remains substantially correct, but the picture has become more nuanced. Full battery-electric vehicles are still broadly hostile to HF operation. The high-voltage inverters and motor controllers produce broadband noise across the entire HF spectrum, and no amount of filtering has consistently solved the problem. If HF is your priority, a full EV is still not the vehicle you want.

Plug-in hybrids (PHEVs) are a mixed bag. In engine-on mode, with the internal combustion engine running and the electric drive system in a supporting role, the noise environment is often manageable with standard suppression techniques. In pure EV mode, however, the inverters are doing all the work and the noise floor climbs accordingly. If you drive a PHEV, plan on operating HF with the engine running.

Mild hybrids — vehicles with 48-volt assist systems that cannot operate in pure electric mode — are generally manageable with standard RFI suppression. The 48V system runs intermittently and at lower power levels than a full hybrid drivetrain.

VHF and UHF remain workable on all of these platforms. The noise is predominantly an HF problem.

One development worth noting: digital modes like FT8 and FT4 can decode signals well below the noise floor. A modest mobile station running FT8 can make contacts that would be flatly impossible on SSB in the same noise environment. This does not make an EV a good HF platform — it makes an EV a tolerable HF platform for digital modes, and only while parked. Operating digital modes while driving is unsafe and in most cases impractical.

⚠ WARNING

High-voltage cables in hybrid and electric vehicles are identified by orange insulation. These carry 200 to 800 volts DC. They are lethal. Do not route radio wiring near them, do not tap into them, do not disturb them. If you are not sure whether a cable is high-voltage, check the service manual. This is non-negotiable.

Smart Charging and Start-Stop

The assumption that your vehicle electrical system sits at a steady 13.8 volts with the engine running is no longer safe. Many late-model vehicles use smart alternators (also called variable-voltage or demand-based charging) that vary output between roughly 12.4 and 15.5 volts depending on battery state-of-charge, electrical load, and engine operating conditions. Your radio may see different supply voltages from one stoplight to the next.

Start-stop systems compound the problem. At every red light, the engine shuts off. The alternator stops. Voltage drops to battery resting voltage. When the light turns green, the engine restarts with a transient that can reset radios, glitch antenna controllers, and produce a burst of broadband noise. For HF operation, start-stop is a nuisance at best and a genuine problem at worst.

Most vehicles with start-stop provide a button to disable it, and you should use it during HF operation. For a more permanent solution, the options include a LiFePO4 auxiliary battery to buffer voltage swings, a DC-DC converter to provide regulated 13.8V regardless of vehicle bus voltage, or in some cases, alternator reprogramming through the vehicle's diagnostic interface. The right approach depends entirely on your specific vehicle — consult a service manual and, if necessary, a qualified automotive electrician.

Battery Junction Boxes and Intelligent Sensors

On many current-production vehicles, you cannot simply clamp a power lead onto the battery terminal the way you could twenty years ago. The battery terminals may be enclosed inside a Battery Junction Box — a fuse block housing that covers the positive terminal and routes multiple circuits through integral fuses and relays. The negative terminal may incorporate an Intelligent Battery Sensor (IBS) that monitors current flow for the vehicle's energy management system. Bypassing or disturbing this sensor can set fault codes and confuse the charging system.

The solution is straightforward but requires homework: read the service manual for your specific vehicle. Identify the correct connection points for auxiliary loads. Route your power leads to those points, not to wherever you can get a clamp to fit. Every vehicle is different, and guessing is a recipe for blown fuses, fault codes, or worse. See the Wiring article for additional detail on modern vehicle power connections.

Digital Modes and POTA/SOTA

The rise of digital modes — FT8 and FT4 in particular — has meaningfully changed what is achievable from a mobile station. These modes decode signals that are 20 dB or more below the noise floor, which means contacts that would be impossible on SSB become routine on FT8. A 100-watt mobile station with a compromise antenna can work genuine DX on FT8 under conditions where SSB would produce nothing but static.

This matters for mobile operators because it partially offsets the inherent disadvantages of mobile HF — small antennas, high noise floors, and limited power. It does not eliminate those disadvantages, and it does not excuse sloppy installation practices, but it expands what is possible.

The Parks on the Air (POTA) program has also created a massive community of mobile and portable operators. If you are setting up a mobile station today, there is a good chance POTA activations are part of your plan. The good news is that POTA operation is typically done while parked, which eliminates the safety concerns of operating while driving and allows the use of digital modes that require a keyboard and screen.

⚠ WARNING

Operate digital modes only while parked. FT8 and FT4 require reading a screen, clicking on decodes, and managing a logging program. This is not something you can do while driving. Pull over, shut off the engine if noise is an issue, and operate from a stationary position. The contact will still be there in 15 seconds.
