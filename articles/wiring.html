Wiring

Last updated: March 2026
Based on original work by Alan Applegate, K0BG Preserved & expanded for the amateur radio community
⚠ Electrical Safety Notice

Vehicle electrical work involves risk of fire, shock, and equipment damage. Incorrect fusing or wiring can cause thermal runaway in wiring harnesses. Never work on vehicle wiring near fuel lines or in enclosed spaces without adequate ventilation. Verify all specifications against your specific vehicle's service manual. If you are not confident in your electrical skills, consult a qualified 12V installer or automotive electrician.

The Cardinal Rule

Let me start with the single most important rule in all of mobile radio wiring. Memorize it. Tattoo it on your forearm if you have to.

⚠ WARNING

Never, ever, use existing vehicle wiring to power any radio gear. Not the cigarette lighter socket. Not an accessory tap. Not a fuse panel piggyback. Not the conveniently-located power wire behind the dash. Never. Run dedicated leads directly from the battery — both positive AND negative.

I cannot overstate how many mobile installation problems trace back to this single mistake. The number of emails I received over the years from hams complaining about alternator whine, ignition noise, or mysterious RFI — only to discover they had their transceiver plugged into a cigarette lighter socket — would fill a book. A thick one.

Your vehicle's factory wiring is designed to carry DC current for vehicle systems. It is routed alongside computer bus wires, sensor leads, and actuator cables. It shares ground paths with dozens of noisy loads. It passes through connectors that were never designed with RF in mind. When you tap into this wiring for radio power, you inject every bit of that electrical filth directly into your transceiver. You also create the opportunity for your transmitted RF to find its way back into the vehicle's electronics, which the vehicle manufacturer will not be amused about and neither will you.

The negative lead is just as important as the positive. Many hams dutifully run a dedicated positive lead to the battery, then connect the negative lead to the nearest body bolt. This is a mistake — you need a clean, dedicated, low-impedance return path that doesn't share current with windshield wiper motors, blower fans, or heated seats. A sloppy ground invites ground loops that couple noise into the receiver.

On older vehicles (roughly pre-2017), the traditional advice is correct: run the negative lead directly to the battery negative terminal. On modern vehicles equipped with an Intelligent Battery Sensor (IBS) on the negative terminal, this approach can cause problems. See the Modern Vehicle Considerations section below for the updated guidance.

Wire Gauge: Getting It Right

Wire gauge matters, and the correct gauge depends on two factors: the current draw of your equipment and the length of the cable run. Both matter. A wire that's adequate for an 8-foot run may be completely inadequate for a 14-foot run in a full-size pickup truck.

🔌 Wire & Fuse Sizing Calculator →

A typical 100-watt HF transceiver draws approximately 22 amps on transmit. That's the number you design around — not the receive current, not the average current, but the peak transmit current. Here are the practical guidelines:

  • 10 AWG — Adequate for cable runs under 10 feet at 100W transceiver current levels.
  • 8 AWG — The minimum for cable runs over 10 feet. This covers most pickup trucks and full-size SUVs.
  • 6 AWG or larger — Required if you're running, or planning to run, an HF amplifier.
💡 TIP

If there is any chance you will add an amplifier later, wire for it now. Pulling new cable through a vehicle that's already assembled is miserable work. Run 6 AWG from the start, even if you're only running barefoot today. The incremental cost of heavier wire is trivial compared to the labor of doing it twice.

Undersized wire doesn't just waste power as heat — though it does that too. It causes voltage drop under load. Your transceiver is designed to operate at 13.8 volts. At 22 amps through undersized wire over a long run, you can easily lose a volt or more. The radio's power output drops, the finals work harder, and in extreme cases the radio's protection circuits start cycling. I have seen installations where the transceiver was barely putting out 60 watts because the wiring couldn't deliver adequate voltage under load. The owner thought his radio was broken. It wasn't. His wiring was.

Calculating Voltage Drop

There's a straightforward formula for calculating actual voltage drop before you pull any wire. Use it. Guessing costs money and frustration.

🔑 KEY CONCEPT

Voltage drop formula: (Rw × 2L × 0.001) + 2k) × A = Vd

Where: Rw = resistance per 1,000 feet from ARRL Handbook wire table; L = one-way cable length in feet; k = connector resistance (typically 0.001Ω each); A = current in amps; Vd = voltage drop in volts.

ARRL Handbook resistance values (ohms per 1,000 feet): #12 = 1.588Ω, #10 = 0.9987Ω, #8 = 0.6281Ω, #6 = 0.3952Ω, #4 = 0.2485Ω, #2 = 0.1563Ω

For reference: factory vehicle power cabling typically drops about 0.6V at 22 amps. That is the baseline you're competing with — your dedicated cable should not add significantly to it. A total drop (factory cable plus your run) of more than 1.0V at transmit current is too much.

Fusing

The positive lead must be fused, and the fuse must be located as close to the battery as physically possible. Not at the radio end. Not in the middle. At the battery. The purpose of the fuse is to protect the wire, not the radio. If the wire shorts to the vehicle body anywhere along its run, the fuse blows before the wire melts and starts a fire. If the fuse is at the radio end, the entire length of wire between the battery and the fuse is unprotected.

🔑 KEY CONCEPT

A fuse protects the wire, not the equipment. Size the fuse to the wire gauge, and place it as close to the battery's positive terminal as possible. The radio has its own internal fusing or protection circuits for the radio itself.

Fuse types in common use for mobile installations:

  • ATC blade fuses — The correct choice for radio power cable inline fuse holders. The ATC fuse element is completely sealed in plastic, making it moisture-resistant. Available up to 40 amps.
  • ATO blade fuses — These look identical to ATC fuses and are what you'll grab at any auto parts store if you're not paying attention. The ATO element is open — not sealed — and is susceptible to moisture and contamination. Inline fuse holders in the power cable run are not waterproof. Use ATC, not ATO, for your radio power fusing.
  • Maxi fuses — Larger blade-style fuses rated up to 80 amps. Use these for amplifier installations or multi-radio setups. Maxi fuse holders are available at any auto parts store.
  • J-Case fuses — A high-current blade fuse format increasingly found in modern vehicle fuse blocks, rated 20–120 amps. These are not interchangeable with Maxi fuses despite similar size. Using the wrong format in a vehicle fuse block can result in a fuse that appears to seat but makes poor contact. Check your service manual before substituting. J-Case fuses also have an extended I²T characteristic, meaning they take longer to open under a given overload — another reason not to use them for amateur radio applications.
  • 5AG (cartridge) fuses — The old round glass or ceramic tube fuses, available up to about 50 amps. These are getting hard to find, and the holders are becoming scarce. If you have a working setup with 5AG fuses there's no reason to change, but for a new installation, blade fuses are the better choice.

Size the fuse appropriately. For a single 100W transceiver on 10 AWG wire, a 30-amp fuse is typical. Don't use a 50-amp fuse "just in case" — oversized fuses defeat the purpose of having one. The fuse must blow before the wire reaches its current-carrying limit.

🔑 FUSE VOLTAGE DROP VALUES

Online voltage drop calculators do not account for fuse and fuse holder resistance. At rated load, a 30-amp ATC fuse has a voltage drop of 95 mV (0.095 volts). A 60-amp Maxi fuse has a voltage drop of 77 mV (0.077 volts) at rated load. Most power cables have two fuses (positive and negative leads), so double these values for your total calculation. If you don't know the exact resistance of a fuse and its holder, use a conservative value of 0.002 ohms per fuse as an approximation. Anderson Power Pole connections add approximately 0.002 volts per connection at rated load.

⚠ CIRCUIT BREAKERS ARE NOT A SUBSTITUTE

If you use circuit breakers instead of fuses, understand this: circuit breakers exhibit a much longer I²T than any fuse except specially designed slow-blow fuses. Worse, most circuit breakers will fail closed on dead shorts if the current exceeds about 2,000 amps. A standard SLI battery in good condition can easily supply 3,000 amps to a dead short — an AGM battery as much as 4,000 amps. This includes so-called marine and aircraft circuit breakers. If you use circuit breakers as switches, fine — but the main wiring protection device should always be a properly-sized fuse.

🔑 KEY CONCEPT

Fuses are I²T devices, not on/off switches. A 30-amp fuse will carry 40 amps for about 8 minutes before blowing. The same fuse will blow in roughly 3 seconds at 100 amps. This time-delay characteristic is intentional — it allows for normal startup surges without nuisance trips. It also means your wire can be damaged by a sustained moderate overcurrent well before the fuse actually opens. Don't interpret a fuse that hasn't blown as evidence that everything is fine. If your wire is getting warm under normal operating loads, the fuse is the wrong size, the wire is the wrong gauge, or both.

Connectors: Anderson Powerpoles

For the interconnections between your power cable and your equipment, Anderson Powerpole connectors are the standard and for good reason. They are polarized, so you can't reverse polarity. They have no exposed pins or blades that can short against the vehicle body. They lock together positively but disconnect easily when you need to remove equipment. They're rated for the currents involved. And they've been adopted as the standard by ARES, RACES, and most emergency communications organizations, which means your gear can plug into anyone's power distribution setup.

The PP45 (45-amp) size is correct for most HF transceiver installations. Larger PP75 and PP120 sizes are available for amplifier feeds.

💡 TIP

When crimping Powerpole contacts, use the proper Anderson crimping tool or a calibrated ratcheting crimper. A pair of pliers and enthusiasm is not a crimping tool. A bad crimp is worse than a bad solder joint — it will develop resistance over time, cause voltage drop, generate heat, and eventually fail. If you can't crimp properly, solder the contacts. Anderson says not to, but a good solder joint is vastly superior to a bad crimp.

One color convention note: the ARES/RACES standard uses red and black housings for positive and negative DC power connections, and you should follow that convention for your radio's power leads. Reserve red and black exclusively for DC power. For motor leads, control wiring, or actuator connections, use a different color pair—yellow and blue, for example—so that motor leads are visually distinct from power wiring and cannot be accidentally cross-connected.

Cable Routing

Where you route your power cables through the vehicle matters enormously. Poor routing is an invitation for noise pickup, RFI problems, and potentially dangerous interactions with vehicle safety systems.

What to Avoid

  • ECU and vehicle computer wiring — The engine control unit and its associated wiring harness carry sensitive low-level signals. Route your power cables well away from these. In most vehicles, the ECU is located on the firewall or under the dash. Find it and avoid it.
  • ABS wiring — The anti-lock brake system uses wheel speed sensors with low-level signals that are susceptible to RF interference. Keep your cables away from ABS wiring, particularly the sensor leads that run to each wheel.
  • Airbag wiring — This deserves its own special emphasis. Airbag circuits are designed to be sensitive to electrical signals — that's how they work. Routing RF-carrying cables near airbag wiring could, in a worst case, cause inadvertent deployment. The airbag wiring in most vehicles is bundled in yellow conduit specifically so you can identify and avoid it. Do so.
  • Fuel lines — Not an electrical concern, but a physical safety one. Don't route cables where they could chafe against fuel lines. A short circuit near a fuel line is the kind of problem that makes the evening news.
⚠ WARNING

Airbag wiring is identified by its yellow conduit or yellow connectors. Never route radio power or coax cables alongside, across, or through airbag wiring bundles. An inadvertent airbag deployment while driving can cause a serious accident and injury.

The best routing path in most vehicles is along the rocker panel or door sill area, under the carpet and trim. This path typically avoids the major electronic modules and provides good physical protection for the cable. Use grommets where cables pass through the firewall, and secure the cable every 12 to 18 inches with cable ties or adhesive-backed mounts so it doesn't dangle, vibrate, or chafe.

💡 TIP

When drilling a new hole through the firewall, a step drill bit cuts clean holes in sheet metal without walking or tearing. For a professional result, a Rotacut (a specialized rotary punch tool) creates a perfectly clean, round hole in sheet metal without drilling — no sharp edges or flanges to clean up afterward. In any case, always check both sides of the firewall before drilling — fuel lines, brake lines, and wiring harnesses often run directly against the firewall on the engine side where they can't be seen from inside the vehicle. Brake lines are commonly attached to the engine side of the firewall directly below the brake pedal. Once the hole is drilled, install a 3/4-inch ID grommet (available at any well-stocked hardware store). To make pulling the wire easier, apply a small amount of wire lubricant (Ideal #77 or equivalent) to the cable's outer surface. Feed the wire from the passenger compartment to the under-hood fuse blocks first, then to its final destination — in almost all cases the amount of wire needed from the firewall outward is much less than that needed behind it.

Most modern vehicles have a fresh air inlet plenum just behind the hood, sometimes containing the windshield wiper assembly and cabin air filter. For this reason, the upper area of the firewall should be avoided. Choose a spot outside any area where the wire might be stepped on or pinched — the closer to the left kick panel (on left-hand drive vehicles) the better.

At connections and terminations, never use twist-on wire nuts (the plastic caps used in residential electrical work). They are not designed for vibration, and they will loosen and fall off in a vehicle. Use proper crimp terminals, or solder-and-heatshrink at every connection.

Insulation, Stranding, and Wire Types

Not all wire is created equal, and choosing the wrong type for a mobile installation is a mistake that will eventually announce itself as a failure.

⚠ NEVER USE SOLID WIRE IN A VEHICLE

Solid wire (single-strand) should never be used in a mobile installation. This includes CAT5 cable, which is sometimes used as a cheaper alternative to factory-supplied modular cables. All solid conductor wire will work-harden under vibration — which is always present in a mobile — and it should be obvious what will happen when it fails. Twist-on wire-nuts should also never be used as splices. They are designed for solid wire only, and their use on stranded wire is a vehicle fire in the making. Think thermolysis.

Insulation temperature ratings matter in a vehicle environment. The insulation temperature rating should be at least 90°C (195°F) in the passenger compartment and 105°C (220°F) in the engine compartment. All exposed wire should be covered with protective split loom regardless of the quality or type of wire used.

On strand count: construction grade THHN and THWN wire has an outer nylon jacket making it very abrasion resistant, but it is typically 7 or 19 strand and rather stiff. Automotive wire is usually 49 strand, much easier to work with, and will generally stay where you put it. High strand count "super-flex" wire like that sold at mobile sound stores is almost too flexible. Avoid after-market sound system wire insulated with plain polyethylene — it is moisture resistant but has poor heat and flame resistance. Do not use it for under-hood wiring.

One more note on super-flex wire including high strand count welding cable: you must use crimp-type connectors to properly terminate it. Soldering these with an iron requires a very large iron, and even then the solder wicks badly, creating more problems than it solves. This is not a concern with 49-strand automotive wire if you know how to solder.

The Twisted-Pair Myth

🔥 MYTH BUSTED

You do not need to twist your positive and negative power leads together. This advice gets repeated endlessly on forums, usually by people who heard it from someone who heard it from someone. The reasoning goes: "CAT5 cable is twisted, so twisting must reject noise." Wrong. CAT5 cable uses twisted pairs for differential signaling, where the signal is the difference between the two conductors. DC power is not a differential signal. Twisting heavy-gauge power leads together accomplishes nothing useful and may damage the insulation. Just run them side by side.

Aluminum-Bodied Vehicles

The increasing use of aluminum body panels (Ford F-150 starting in 2015, various others since) introduces a specific concern. Aluminum and copper are galvanically incompatible. When dissimilar metals are in contact in the presence of moisture, galvanic corrosion will destroy the joint — and it's the aluminum that loses.

For aluminum-bodied vehicles:

  • Use aluminum mounting hardware (bolts, nuts, washers) for any electrical connections to aluminum body panels.
  • Follow the vehicle manufacturer's guidelines for grounding and bonding points. These vehicles often have specific factory-designated grounding locations that use compatible hardware.
  • Apply anti-corrosion compound (such as Penetrox or No-Ox-Id) to any connections between dissimilar metals that can't be avoided.
  • Inspect bonding and grounding connections annually for signs of corrosion.

Modern Vehicle Considerations (2017+)

Much of the mobile installation advice in the amateur radio community — including the original K0BG material — was written when vehicles had simple batteries with exposed terminals and alternators that charged at a fixed 14.4 volts. Modern vehicles are significantly more complex, and some of the classic advice needs updating.

Battery-Mounted Fuse Blocks

Many modern vehicles — GM trucks from ~2019+, Ford F-150s and Explorers, Stellantis products — have a Battery-Mounted Fuse Block (BMFB) or Battery Junction Box (BJB) integrated directly onto the positive battery terminal. This is a plastic housing containing high-current mega fuses (100A–300A) that protect the vehicle's main wiring harnesses. On some vehicles, the battery positive terminal is completely inaccessible underneath this assembly.

🔑 KEY CONCEPT

The mega fuses in the BMFB protect the vehicle's wiring from catastrophic shorts. They do NOT protect your accessory wire. A 150-amp mega fuse will not blow for a 30-amp short on your 10 AWG radio wire — your wire will melt and potentially start a fire long before that mega fuse trips. You still need your own correctly-sized inline fuse immediately after your connection point.

Some BMFB units have an auxiliary stud terminal specifically for accessories. If yours has one, you can connect your positive lead there with a ring terminal, then install your inline fuse within a few inches. If there is no auxiliary stud, you may need to add an aftermarket fuse distribution block that clamps alongside the BMFB, or access the battery terminal directly if mechanically possible. Consult your vehicle's service manual for the specific layout.

Intelligent Battery Sensors (IBS)

This is the single biggest change that affects mobile radio installations in modern vehicles. Most vehicles from roughly 2017 onward (GM, Ford, Stellantis, and nearly all European makes since ~2012) have an Intelligent Battery Sensor clamped to or integrated into the negative battery terminal. It measures current flow, voltage, and temperature, and reports to the Body Control Module (BCM).

The BCM uses this data to manage the charging system, control start-stop operation, and shed electrical loads if the battery gets low. Here is the problem: if you connect your radio's negative lead directly to the battery negative post — above the IBS — the sensor cannot see that current. As far as the car's computer is concerned, current is leaving through the positive terminal but not coming back. This mismatch can cause:

  • Diagnostic trouble codes (DTCs) — battery sensor rationality errors
  • Erratic charging — alternator overcharges or undercharges because SOC calculations are wrong
  • Start-stop system disabling itself
  • Premature load shedding — the BCM shuts off accessories because it thinks the battery is dying
  • Check engine or battery warning lights
⚠ WARNING

On IBS-equipped vehicles, do not connect the negative lead directly to the battery negative post. This bypasses the current sensor and confuses the vehicle's battery management system. BMW and Mercedes are notoriously sensitive to this. GM trucks are more tolerant but will still log codes.

The updated recommendation for the negative lead on IBS-equipped vehicles: Connect to a factory ground stud on the engine block or inner fender, near the battery. These are robust, low-impedance ground points that are part of the vehicle's main ground path. All current flowing through chassis ground passes through the IBS on its way back to the battery, so the sensor can properly account for your radio's current draw.

This is a departure from the original K0BG advice, but it is necessary on modern vehicles. The key nuance: a factory ground stud on the engine block within a few feet of the battery is a very different thing from a random body bolt in the trunk. The impedance difference between a short run to a factory engine block stud and a direct battery terminal connection is negligible at DC. The noise problems K0BG warned about came from using thin chassis sheet metal as a return path over long distances — that is still bad advice. A factory ground stud is not.

💡 TIP

How to tell if your vehicle has an IBS: look at the negative battery terminal. If there is a small electronic module with a wiring connector clamped between the cable end and the battery post, that is the IBS. If the cable bolts directly to the post with no electronics in between, you don't have one and the classic "both leads to the battery" advice still applies.

Smart Alternators and Variable Voltage

Modern vehicles do not maintain a constant 13.8-volt charging voltage. The ECM commands the alternator field current based on battery state of charge, engine load, and fuel economy targets. GM calls this Load Response Control (LRC). Ford calls it Smart Charging. The practical result:

  • During deceleration/braking: Up to 15.0–15.5V (regenerative charging to recapture energy)
  • Normal driving: 13.2–14.4V (varies with battery SOC)
  • Steady cruise, battery full: Can drop to 12.4–13.0V (alternator partially decouples to save fuel)
  • After a start-stop restart: Brief burst to 14.8V+ to replenish quickly

Most modern transceivers (Icom IC-7300, Yaesu FT-891, Kenwood TS-480, etc.) are designed for 13.8V ±15% and will handle this range without issue, though output power may dip slightly at the low end. The bigger concern is motorized screwdriver antennas — motor speed varies with voltage, which affects tune times and can confuse turn-counting controllers that assume consistent motor speed. SWR-seeking controllers are less affected.

Start-Stop Systems

Many modern vehicles shut the engine off at stoplights to save fuel. When the engine is off, there is no alternator output — everything runs on the battery alone. If you are transmitting 100 watts during an engine-off period, you are pulling 22 amps straight from the battery with nothing to replenish it.

Worse, when the engine restarts, the starter motor draws 150–300 amps. This causes a brief but severe voltage dip (down to 9–10V) that will reset most transceivers, produce a loud pop in the audio, and may trip internal protection circuits.

💡 TIP

Disable start-stop during HF operation. Most vehicles have a button to disable it per drive cycle. Aftermarket modules (like Autostop Eliminator) can default it to off permanently. For VHF/UHF FM at lower power levels the issue is less severe, but the restart voltage transient is still a concern.

48V Mild Hybrid Systems

Some vehicles (Ram 1500 eTorque, various Mercedes/Audi/BMW models) use a 48-volt mild hybrid system alongside the conventional 12V system. The two are electrically isolated by a DC-DC converter. All ham radio equipment connects to the 12V system only. The 48V wiring is typically identified by blue cables (as opposed to orange for full high-voltage hybrid systems). Leave it alone entirely. The 12V side of these vehicles follows the same IBS and smart alternator guidance above.

Putting It All Together

A proper mobile power installation is not complicated. It's just deliberate. Here's the sequence:

  1. Determine your total current requirement (transceiver plus any amplifier, plus a reasonable margin).
  2. Select wire gauge based on current and run length.
  3. Install an appropriately-sized fuse holder as close to the battery's positive terminal as possible.
  4. Route both positive and negative leads through the firewall (using a grommet) and along the vehicle interior, away from ECU, ABS, airbag, and fuel line routing.
  5. Terminate the positive lead at the battery terminal (or BMFB auxiliary stud if the terminal is inaccessible). For the negative lead: on vehicles without an IBS, connect to the battery negative terminal. On vehicles with an IBS, connect to a factory ground stud on the engine block near the battery. See IBS section above.
  6. At the radio end, terminate with Anderson Powerpole connectors.
  7. Secure the cable run with cable ties every 12 to 18 inches.
  8. Test under load: key the transmitter and measure voltage at the radio. You should see no more than 0.5V drop from the battery terminal voltage.

That's it. No tricks, no shortcuts, no clever hacks. Just solid, properly-sized wire run directly from the battery, fused at the source, routed away from sensitive electronics, and terminated with quality connectors. Do it right once and you'll never think about it again. Do it wrong and you'll be chasing noise, blowing fuses, and wondering why your radio sounds like a garbage disposal.

Summary

The wiring in a mobile installation is the foundation that everything else depends on. A perfect antenna on a perfectly bonded vehicle will still sound terrible if the power wiring couples engine noise into the receiver. Dedicated leads, correct wire gauge, proper fusing, quality connectors, careful routing, and — on modern vehicles — respecting the IBS and battery management system: these aren't optional refinements. They're the baseline. Start here, get it right, and the rest of the installation has a fighting chance.
