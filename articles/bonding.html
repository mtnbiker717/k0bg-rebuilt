Bonding

Last updated: March 2026
Based on original work by Alan Applegate, K0BG Preserved & expanded for the amateur radio community
⚠ Vehicle Modification Notice

Drilling or modifying vehicle bodywork may affect structural integrity, void warranties, and create corrosion points if not properly treated. Check your vehicle service manual before drilling. Avoid drilling near wiring harnesses, fuel lines, airbag components, and structural adhesive joints. In aluminum-body vehicles, use only manufacturer-approved hardware to prevent galvanic corrosion.

In This Article

What Is Bonding, and Why Should You Care?

Bonding is the process of installing grounding straps between the vehicle's frame or unibody structure and all of its bolted-on hardware. Doors, hoods, trunk lids, exhaust systems, bumper backing plates, and truck beds are all examples of components that need to be electrically tied together. If you're running an HF mobile installation and you haven't bonded your vehicle, you're leaving performance on the table. Period.

Let me be blunt about something right up front: bonding is not primarily about DC safety. Your vehicle's 12-volt system works just fine without bonding straps. The starter cranks, the headlights illuminate, and the engine computer does its thing. Modern vehicles are more-or-less DC contiguous through their bolt-on assemblies. But here's the catch that trips up nearly everyone: DC contiguous does not mean RF contiguous. Not even close.

🔑 KEY CONCEPT

The primary function of bonding is to improve the ground plane your vehicle offers to the antenna system. A vehicle body riddled with RF-isolated panels is a lousy ground plane. Bonding ties all those panels together so they behave as a single, unified conductive surface at radio frequencies.

Think about it this way. A bolt through a painted hole, even one torqued to specification, might measure a fraction of an ohm on your multimeter. DC current flows through it without complaint. But at 14 MHz, that same bolted joint looks like a high-impedance mess. Paint, corrosion, oxide layers, rubber grommets, and the sheer inductance of a round bolt all conspire to make these joints invisible to RF. Your vehicle's sheet metal might as well be a collection of unconnected panels as far as your antenna is concerned.

The Exhaust System: A Special Problem

If there's one thing that catches new mobile operators off guard, it's the exhaust system. Your exhaust is thermally and physically isolated from the vehicle body by design. It hangs from rubber isolators. It passes through heat shields. One end may be DC grounded to the engine block (which is bolted to the frame), while the other end — the tailpipe — floats electrically. In RF terms, your exhaust system is a long, resonant conductor dangling underneath the vehicle, coupled to nothing useful and potentially reradiating your signal in unpredictable ways.

The exhaust system is also a spectacular noise generator. Those rubber hangers and heat-shield clamps create intermittent metal-to-metal contact that generates broadband noise as you drive. Every bump in the road turns your exhaust into a spark-gap transmitter. If you've ever heard a rhythmic popping or crackling on HF that varies with road conditions, the exhaust is almost certainly part of the problem.

⚠ WARNING

The exhaust system gets extremely hot. Bond it at every joint and hanger point, but use hardware rated for the thermal environment. Stainless steel hardware is recommended for exhaust bonding. Route straps so they won't contact the exhaust pipe directly along their length, and leave enough slack to accommodate thermal expansion and the normal movement of rubber isolators.

🔑 EXHAUST BONDING PROCEDURE

Almost without exception, modern exhaust systems are made of good-grade stainless steel. Wire-brush a small area for the lug to bite into and use stainless steel hose clamps to secure the lug. Bond the exhaust system at these points:

  1. Before the catalytic converter
  2. After the catalytic converter
  3. At least once after the muffler
  4. A fourth strap at the tailpipe if it is extra long

The opposite end of each exhaust strap should attach to the underframe or unibody strut work. If the car is undercoated, clean a small area with a Dremel tool and wire wheel — but don't brush past the zinc layer to bare metal. Don't rely on any factory strap to provide a good RF ground; they're meant solely for DC grounding of tail lights and are inadequate for RF grounding needs.

What to Bond

Here is the complete list of what needs bonding in a typical vehicle installation. No shortcuts. Do them all.

  • Exhaust system — Bond at every joint and at every hanger point. This is the single most impactful bonding you will do.
  • Hood — Bond across both hinges. The hinge pin and the painted hinge leaves are not reliable RF conductors.
  • Trunk lid — Bond across both hinges, same as the hood.
  • All doors — Bond across each hinge on every door. Yes, all of them. A four-door sedan has eight hinges minimum.
  • Tailgate and lift gates — SUVs and hatchbacks need bonding here too. The gas struts and torsion bars are not conductors.
  • Bumper backing plates — Modern plastic bumper covers hide steel or aluminum reinforcement bars. Bond these to the frame or unibody.
  • Truck bed to cab — On both sides. The truck bed sits on rubber isolators and is electrically floating. This is a big one for pickup truck installations.
  • Frame to body — On body-on-frame vehicles, the body mounts use rubber bushings by design. Bond across them.
  • Engine block to frame — Often there is an OEM ground strap here, but it may not be adequate for RF. Add a proper bonding strap.

Body-on-Frame vs. Unibody

Body-on-frame vehicles — most pickup trucks, older SUVs, and full-size vans — require significantly more bonding work than unibody vehicles. The reason is simple: the body literally sits on rubber bushings atop the frame. Every single body mount is an RF discontinuity. You need bonding straps across every body mount, plus all the doors, hood, and tailgate work common to any vehicle.

Unibody vehicles have it somewhat easier. The roof, pillars, floor pan, and fenders are all welded together into one structure. Doors, hood, and trunk still need bonding, and the exhaust still needs attention, but you don't have the body-to-frame isolation problem. Don't get complacent, though. "Easier" doesn't mean "unnecessary."

Materials and Methods

Use flat braided copper strap, 3/4-inch to 1-inch wide. This is not negotiable.

🔥 MYTH BUSTED

Round wire is not a substitute for flat braid. RF current travels on the outside surface of a conductor due to skin effect. A round wire has the minimum possible surface area for its cross-section. Flat braid maximizes surface area and minimizes inductance. A piece of 8 AWG round wire might look beefy, but it is electrically inferior to a 3/4-inch flat braid for RF bonding purposes.

Keep every bonding strap as short as physically possible. Any strap longer than 10 inches is too long. Inductance increases with length, and at HF frequencies, even a foot-long strap can become a significant impedance. If you can't reach between two points with a 10-inch strap, you need to find a closer bonding point or create an intermediate connection.

🔑 STRAP SIZING GUIDE

A good working set of straps for a typical vehicle installation:

  • 6-inch straps — for doors (one per hinge)
  • 10-inch straps — for both sides of the engine, both hinges of the hood and trunk
  • Three or more straps — for the exhaust system (before the catalytic converter, after the catalytic converter, and at least once after the muffler; a fourth strap at the tailpipe if it is extra long)

Lugs: use two sizes — ones with a 1/4-inch bolt hole (for existing bolts) and ones with a #10 screw hole (for everything else). Wire size depends on braid size: lugs for #12 are adequate for RG8 shield braid; lugs for #4 work best for 1-inch braid material. Always crimp and solder — crimping alone allows moisture to seep into the connection. If the strap is subject to abrasion, cover it with heat shrink tubing.

Connection Technique

⚠ DO NOT SAND TO BARE METAL

Rule one: do not sand the finish down to bare metal before attaching bonding straps. Modern vehicle bodies and frames are dipped in a zinc compound. This zinc compound oxidizes in the presence of air and seals scratches in its surface. If you sand through to bare steel, you have destroyed that protection. Let the star washers do their job of biting through paint layers — the zinc compound will seal the connection. The use of NoOx and similar compounds really isn't necessary, but they do retard rust and oxidation of the mounting hardware. Do not use these compounds under star washers or between the mount and the vehicle's structure — let the zinc oxide do its job.

Use star (serrated) lock washers under bolt heads and under nuts — not standard lock washers. The preferred type has both internal and external teeth. When properly installed, they bite through finish layers and into the base metal. Self-tapping sheet metal screws in #10 size work well if the material is 12 gauge or less; self-drilling screws work better for thicker material. Don't use screws longer than 1/2 inch — 3/8 inch is a better choice. Longer screws won't hold better and might run into something you can't see.

Once installed, a dab of paint or corrosion inhibitor over the connection point will slow future oxidation without compromising the electrical joint. Inspect all bonding connections at least once a year. Five years in a dry climate is enough to produce significant rust on even well-installed hardware.

Where to Get Braid

Flat copper braid is available from several sources:

  • DX Engineering — Sells bonding kits and bulk braid specifically for mobile installations.
  • Ham Radio Outlet (HRO) — Usually stocks bonding braid.
  • Electric Motion EM2080 — A good commercial bonding strap product.
  • Salvaged coax shield — Strip the jacket and center conductor from discarded RG-8 or RG-213 and you have perfectly serviceable bonding braid. Free is a good price.

One thing that is not suitable for bonding: 3M 1181 copper foil tape. It's excellent for shielding and can be useful for other purposes in a mobile installation, but it is not a bonding material. It lacks the mechanical durability and current-carrying capacity needed for structural bonding.

What Bonding Is NOT

Let's clear up some persistent nonsense.

Bonding is not a workaround for improper antenna mounting. If your antenna is mounted on a mirror bracket, a gutter clip, or a magnetic mount sitting on the trunk lid, no amount of bonding will fix the fundamental problem. Bonding improves a ground plane that already works; it doesn't create one from nothing.

⚠ WARNING

Running a ground strap from your antenna mount directly to the vehicle frame is a common but misguided practice. Doing so can actually exacerbate common mode currents and create ground loop problems. The antenna mount's connection to the vehicle body is the ground connection. Adding a strap to the frame creates an alternate RF path that invites trouble.

⚠ TRUNK-LID ANTENNA MOUNTING

If you mount an HF antenna on a trunk lid, you will have excessive common mode current — no matter how well you bond the lid and/or mount. Making matters worse, common mode chokes must be placed outside the vehicle to minimize RFI ingress. This goes for control lead chokes too. This is a fundamental reason to avoid trunk mounting on any band below 2 meters. If you have resorted to a hatch or trunk lip mount, make sure both the hatch and the coax choke are outside the vehicle, and make sure the hatch is very well bonded. Lack of bonding in these areas is the first place to look when you have common mode problems.

Here is a rule you should commit to memory: if adding a ground strap fixed, reduced, or eliminated an RFI problem, then something else in your installation was already wrong. The strap didn't fix the root cause; it masked a symptom. Maybe the coax shield wasn't properly grounded at the mount. Maybe the antenna mount itself had a poor connection to the body. Maybe there was a common mode current problem on the feedline. Whatever it was, the strap papered over the real deficiency, and it will come back to bite you in other ways.

Real-World Results

For the skeptics — and there are always skeptics — here's what a systematic bonding campaign looks like in practice.

A typical vehicle with no bonding might exhibit an ambient noise floor of S9 or higher on the HF bands while parked with the engine running. The noise is a combination of ignition system hash, alternator whine, and broadband noise from intermittent metal-to-metal contact throughout the vehicle structure.

After bonding the exhaust system and the major body panels (hood, doors, trunk), the noise floor typically drops to S3 or S4. That alone is a dramatic improvement — you've just recovered five or six S-units of usable signal, which translates to the difference between copying a weak station and not hearing it at all.

Continue the bonding work to include every remaining joint, add appropriate ferrite chokes on the power leads and feedline (see the articles on Common Mode Currents and RFI Problems), and the noise floor can drop to S0. Zero. At that point, the only noise you hear is atmospheric and propagation noise — exactly what you'd hear on a well-built home station.

💡 TIP

Do your bonding work systematically and measure the results at each stage. Use a consistent frequency and note the S-meter reading with the engine running and the vehicle in Park. This way you'll know which bonds made the most difference, and you'll have documentation if a bond fails later and noise comes back.

Summary

Bonding is not optional for a serious HF mobile installation. It's not difficult. It's not expensive. It just requires crawling around your vehicle with braid, hardware, and a wire brush, making clean metal-to-metal connections across every bolted joint. The payoff is a dramatically quieter receiver and a more effective antenna system. There is no magic here — just physics, applied with a little effort and attention to detail.

Skip the bonding, and you're operating with one hand tied behind your back. Do it right, and you'll wonder why you didn't do it sooner.

Aluminum, Composites, and the Modern Vehicle

Everything above assumes a vehicle made primarily of stamped steel panels and a steel frame or unibody. That described nearly every vehicle on the road when this site was first written. It no longer does. If you're installing an HF rig in a vehicle built after roughly 2015, you need to understand what your vehicle is actually made of before you reach for the braid and star washers. The physics hasn't changed, but the materials certainly have.

Aluminum-Body Vehicles

Ford switched the F-150 to an aluminum body in 2015, and the industry has been moving in that direction ever since. Aluminum is an excellent conductor — better than steel, actually — so you'd think bonding would be easier. It isn't. Aluminum presents two problems that steel doesn't, and both of them will ruin your bonding work if you ignore them.

Problem one: the oxide layer. Aluminum forms aluminum oxide (Al2O3) the instant it is exposed to air. This oxide layer is extremely hard, extremely thin, and an excellent electrical insulator. You can scrape it off with a wire brush, but it reforms within seconds — literally before you can get the bolt tightened. Star washers alone won't solve this. You need a conductive joint compound such as Penetrox A (or its generic equivalent, an oxide-inhibiting compound loaded with zinc particles) applied to the bare metal before assembly. The compound displaces oxygen, prevents new oxide formation, and the embedded metal particles maintain conductivity through the joint.

Problem two: galvanic corrosion. When two dissimilar metals are in contact in the presence of moisture, you get a battery. Copper and aluminum are far apart on the galvanic series, and copper wins that fight decisively — meaning the aluminum loses material. In a humid environment, bare copper braid bolted directly to aluminum will destroy the aluminum at the joint in a matter of months.

⚠ WARNING

Do not use bare copper braid on aluminum body panels. Galvanic corrosion will eat the aluminum at the contact point, and you'll be left with a corroded, high-impedance mess that is worse than no bond at all. The joint can deteriorate to the point of structural concern in as little as one wet season. Use tin-plated copper braid with stainless steel hardware and oxide-inhibiting compound on every aluminum connection.

🔑 KEY CONCEPT

Tin-plated copper braid is essential for aluminum-body vehicles. The tin plating acts as a galvanic barrier between the copper and the aluminum, dramatically slowing corrosion while maintaining excellent RF conductivity. Combine it with stainless steel hardware and a conductive oxide-inhibiting compound like Penetrox A at every joint. This is not optional on aluminum — it is the only way to make a bond that lasts.

Adhesive-Bonded Panels

Here is something that surprises people who haven't looked under the skin of a modern aluminum vehicle: the panels are not spot-welded together. Spot-welding aluminum is far more difficult and expensive than spot-welding steel, so most aluminum-body vehicles use structural adhesive combined with self-piercing rivets instead. The adhesive carries the structural load; the rivets hold things in position while the adhesive cures and provide additional mechanical fastening.

This matters to you because you must not drill through adhesive-bonded joints. Drilling through a structural adhesive joint compromises the integrity of the bond, can introduce a moisture path that accelerates corrosion from the inside out, and will almost certainly void your vehicle's structural warranty. Don't do it.

Instead, use factory bolt locations for your bonding connections. Every modern vehicle has factory ground studs and bolt points that were designed to accept electrical connections. Your vehicle's service manual identifies every factory ground point — and on aluminum-body trucks and SUVs, these are specifically engineered to handle the galvanic and oxide issues discussed above. Use them.

Non-Conductive Body Panels

Carbon fiber, fiberglass, and sheet molding compound (SMC) are not conductors. They cannot be bonded into your ground plane because they are not part of your ground plane. Period.

This isn't a theoretical concern. The Corvette C8 uses extensive carbon fiber body panels. Several pickup trucks use composite beds — the GMC CarbonPro bed is carbon fiber, and various other manufacturers use SMC or fiberglass for bed panels, fenders, or hoods. These panels are RF-invisible. Your antenna's ground plane simply does not include them.

The practical consequence is a smaller effective ground plane than the vehicle's physical footprint would suggest. A pickup truck with a composite bed and a cab-mounted antenna has a ground plane that ends at the back of the cab, not at the tailgate. You cannot fix this with bonding straps — there is nothing conductive to bond to. What you can do is adjust your expectations. A smaller ground plane means slightly different antenna impedance characteristics and possibly a less uniform radiation pattern. It's not a disaster, but you need to know what you're working with.

Electric and Hybrid Vehicles

Electric and hybrid vehicles change the bonding calculus in two important ways.

First, there is no exhaust system to bond. On a pure EV, the single most impactful bonding target — the one I told you to do first, the one that drops the noise floor by multiple S-units — simply doesn't exist. That's the good news.

The bad news is that the noise environment is different, not absent. The motor inverter converts DC battery voltage to three-phase AC to drive the traction motor, and it does so by switching power transistors at high frequency. The result is broadband RF hash — a steady, buzzing, hissing interference that is a different character entirely from the crackle-and-pop of an internal combustion vehicle. It tends to be more uniform and harder to localize. Proper bonding of body panels still helps reduce the vehicle's susceptibility to this noise, and ferrite suppression on the 12-volt accessory bus becomes even more important.

Doors, hood, trunk or liftgate, and any other bolted-on metal panels still need bonding exactly as described in the sections above. The ground plane argument hasn't changed just because the powertrain has.

⚠ WARNING

Do NOT attach bonding straps to any high-voltage component or to any cable with orange jacketing. Orange cables in EVs and hybrids carry hundreds of volts DC. The high-voltage battery, motor, inverter, and charging system are isolated from the vehicle chassis by design, and for very good reason. Interfering with HV isolation can kill you. Bonding work on EVs and hybrids is limited to the 12-volt chassis and body panels — the same sheet metal you'd bond on any other vehicle. Leave the orange cables alone.

Factory Ground Points

Modern vehicles have documented factory ground studs — typically located on inner fenders, the radiator support, the firewall, and the engine block. These are purpose-built electrical connection points with corrosion protection already engineered in. On aluminum and mixed-material vehicles, the factory ground studs are often the only points where the manufacturer has addressed the oxide and galvanic issues properly from the start.

Using factory ground points is almost always better than drilling new holes, especially on aluminum or mixed-material vehicles. Drilling creates bare aluminum surfaces that immediately begin to oxidize, introduces a moisture entry point, and on adhesive-bonded structures may compromise a structural joint. The factory points avoid all of these problems.

💡 TIP

Your vehicle's factory service manual (not the owner's manual — the actual shop manual) contains a wiring diagram section that identifies every factory ground point by location and stud size. For most vehicles, this information is also available through services like AllData or Mitchell. Spend the time to find these points before you start drilling. On an aluminum-body truck, the factory ground studs may be all you need.
