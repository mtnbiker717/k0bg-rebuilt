Installation Notes

Last updated: March 2026
Based on original work by Alan Applegate, K0BG Preserved & expanded for the amateur radio community
⚠ Installation Safety Notice

Improper radio installation can interfere with vehicle safety systems including ABS, electronic power steering, airbag deployment, and ADAS sensors. RFI ingress into vehicle control systems can cause unpredictable behavior while driving. Always verify your installation does not affect safety-critical systems before driving. If safety systems malfunction after installation, stop transmitting immediately and diagnose the cause before resuming operation.

In This Article

The Hard Truth About Mobile Installation

Let me say this plainly: the performance of your mobile HF station is directly dependent on the time, effort, and expense you're willing to invest. There are no shortcuts. There are no magic bullets. There is no miracle product at the hamfest flea market that will turn a sloppy installation into a good one. If you want it to work well, you have to do it right, and doing it right takes work.

Very few amateurs go shopping for a vehicle with antenna mounting in mind. Most of us buy a car or truck for practical reasons — commuting, hauling the family, towing a boat — and then try to figure out how to bolt radio equipment onto it after the fact. This is understandable. It is also the beginning of most problems.

🔑 KEY CONCEPT

Notify your insurance company before you start. Check your auto insurance policy for requirements around permanently installed aftermarket equipment. Some insurers require disclosure; others do not. If your equipment is damaged in an accident or stolen, and you have not disclosed it, the insurer may decline coverage on the radio gear. The premium impact of disclosure is typically negligible — far less than the replacement cost of an HF transceiver. Make the call before you drill the first hole.

Vehicle Selection and Its Consequences

Some vehicles are simply harder to work with than others. Pickup trucks with metal bodies and a solid roof remain the gold standard for mobile HF. A steel roof provides a ground plane directly under the antenna, a bed or trunk offers space for equipment, and there is usually a straightforward path for wiring.

Then there is everything else. Minivans, SUVs, Jeeps, station wagons, crossovers, and vehicles with plastic body panels present the greatest challenges. The reasons are numerous: fiberglass or plastic roof panels with no metal underneath, limited interior space, complex headliner assemblies, and vehicle designs that seem to actively resist modification.

🔑 KEY CONCEPT

It is the metal mass directly under the antenna that counts — not what is alongside it, not what is behind it, and not what is in front of it. A steel roof with an antenna mounted dead center is ideal. A plastic roof with sheet metal somewhere in the general vicinity is not a substitute, no matter how many forum posts claim otherwise.

If your vehicle has a fiberglass or composite roof panel — and many modern SUVs and crossovers do — you have a fundamental problem that no amount of bonding straps will solve. You need metal under the antenna. Period. This sometimes means fabricating a ground plane, which is a topic unto itself. More often, it means accepting a mounting location that offers adequate metal mass even if it is not the highest point on the vehicle.

Yes, You Will Need to Drill Holes

This is where I lose about half my audience. Typically, a proper installation requires drilling holes in your vehicle. There, I said it. I can already hear the sharp intake of breath from some readers. Allow me to address this directly.

I have encountered amateurs who go to extraordinary lengths to avoid drilling a single hole in their vehicle. They use magnetic mounts on a cookie sheet. They clamp antennas to luggage racks with hose clamps. They stick transceivers to dashboards with Velcro. The use of Velcro, in particular, demonstrates a level of dedication towards the preservation of their cars to such an extent that they would rather risk injury or death than drill any holes. Think about that for a moment. A Velcro-mounted transceiver becomes a projectile in a collision. A properly through-bolted mount does not.

⚠ WARNING

An unsecured transceiver weighing 5 to 10 pounds becomes a deadly projectile in a 30 mph collision. The forces involved are enormous. Velcro, suction cups, and friction mounts are not adequate restraints. Mount your equipment properly or accept the consequences.

Your vehicle is a tool. It depreciates every day whether you drill holes in it or not. A properly installed NMO mount with a dab of silicone sealant will not cause rust, will not leak, and will not materially affect the resale value of your vehicle. You know what does affect resale value? A cracked windshield from a transceiver that flew off the dashboard. But I digress.

Get a Repair Manual

Before you touch a single wire or drill a single hole, buy a repair manual for your specific vehicle. A factory service manual from the dealer will run $75 to $125, but it contains everything: wiring diagrams, torque specifications, component locations, and the information you need to avoid damaging something expensive. An aftermarket manual from Haynes or Chilton costs roughly $25 and covers the basics adequately for most installations.

Why is this important? Because modern vehicles are crammed with wiring, sensors, control modules, and other components that you can easily damage if you do not know where they are. Drilling through a roof pillar without checking the manual first is a fine way to sever an airbag harness — and that is a mistake you really do not want to make.

Airbag Safety — This Is Not Optional Reading

Modern vehicles contain Supplemental Restraint Systems (SRS) — airbags — in locations that may surprise you. Side curtain airbags run along the headliner edges. Their wiring often routes through the roof pillars (A-pillar, B-pillar, C-pillar). Knee airbags hide under the dashboard. Some vehicles have airbags in the seats themselves.

⚠ WARNING

Airbags are explosive devices. The inflators contain propellant charges that deploy with enough force to cause serious injury or death if triggered outside their housings. Never probe, cut, splice, or route wiring near SRS components without consulting the factory service manual. If you are apprehensive about working near airbag components — and a healthy respect for them is entirely reasonable — seek professional help. This is not a place for guesswork.

SRS wiring is typically enclosed in yellow conduit or connectors to distinguish it from other vehicle wiring. Learn to recognize it. Stay away from it. If your planned cable routing requires passing near SRS wiring, find another route.

⚠ WARNING

Seat-mounted side airbags have their own internal capacitor power supply. Disconnecting the vehicle battery does NOT prevent a seat airbag from deploying. The capacitor inside the airbag module maintains enough energy to fire the inflator for up to several minutes after battery disconnection — and some modules retain charge longer. Before working near seat-mounted airbags or routing wiring through the seat area, consult the factory service manual for the specific discharge time for your vehicle. The manual will also identify which seating positions have airbag inflators and where they are located in the seat assembly.

Transceiver Mounting — Where and How

Choosing a location for your transceiver involves balancing several competing requirements: accessibility for operating, adequate ventilation, structural support for mounting, and safety in the event of a collision.

Let me first address what not to do. Do not use cubby holes, center consoles, headrest supports, or storage boxes as transceiver mounting locations. I see this constantly, and it is wrong for one simple reason: transceivers need airflow. A transceiver stuffed into an enclosed cubby hole with no ventilation will overheat. Modern transceivers have thermal protection that will reduce output power or shut down entirely when overheated. You did not buy a 100-watt radio to have it throttle itself to 25 watts because you could not be bothered to mount it properly.

💡 TIP

Always use the factory-supplied mounting bracket for your transceiver. The manufacturer designed it to provide proper heat dissipation and secure mounting. Aftermarket brackets, homemade solutions, and "creative" mounting schemes are almost always inferior.

⚠ WARNING

Trunk temperatures in summer can reach 180°F or more in direct sunlight on a dark-colored vehicle. Most amateur transceivers are specified to operate to 140°F (60°C). A transceiver body installed in the trunk can be subjected to temperatures above its specifications before you ever turn the key. This matters even if the radio appears to survive: high temperatures accelerate electrolytic capacitor aging and degrade every semiconductor junction in the unit. If your transceiver body must be in the trunk, mount it against the rear seat bulkhead where temperatures are lower — not against the spare tire well where they are highest.

On the subject of heat management: do not install an auxiliary fan blowing on the transceiver heat sink. This sounds counterintuitive, but there is a good reason. The engineers who designed your radio calculated the heat dissipation requirements and designed the heat sink accordingly. An external fan blowing ambient air — which in a vehicle interior can easily be 130 degrees Fahrenheit or more in summer — across the heat sink does nothing useful. Worse, it can blow dust and debris into the radio. The transceiver has its own internal fan if it needs one. Trust the design.

Resist the Temptation to Over-Install

New mobile operators have a tendency to install every piece of equipment they own, whether they need it or not. Here is a good rule of thumb: if you do not need it for the mode you are operating, do not install it.

An SWR/wattmeter permanently plumbed into the feed line of an FM radio is a classic example. What, exactly, are you monitoring? FM is constant-carrier. The SWR is not going to change while you are driving down the highway. All that meter is doing is introducing another connection point (read: potential failure point), adding loss, and taking up space. If you need to check SWR, check it during setup and put the meter away.

🔥 MYTH BUSTED

More equipment does not equal a better station. Every unnecessary component adds weight, draws power, introduces potential failure points, and clutters your operating position. Install what you need. Leave the rest at home.

Truck Bed Boxes as Antenna Mounts

Truck bed-mounted toolboxes and storage boxes are popular antenna mounting locations, and they can work — with caveats. The box itself is typically aluminum or steel and sits in the bed of the truck, which seems like a reasonable platform for an antenna mount.

However, these boxes often sit on rubber or plastic pads that electrically isolate them from the truck bed. If you mount an antenna on an isolated box, that box is not part of the vehicle ground plane. You must bond the box to the bed — metal to metal, with proper hardware, paint removed at the contact points. See the Bonding article for details.

Additionally, do not mount the antenna in the center of the box lid. Mount it at the rear edge or a corner, as close to the truck bed metal as possible. Remember the fundamental rule: it is the metal under the antenna that matters. The aluminum lid of a toolbox is not a ground plane. The steel truck bed, properly bonded to the box, is.

⚠ NEVER MOUNT TRANSCEIVERS INSIDE A BED BOX

There is one mounting location you do not want to use for radio equipment: inside a bed-mounted storage box, no matter what it is made of. The reason is moisture condensation. Storage boxes are not vented — they're made to be weatherproof. In most areas with large variations in moisture and temperature, condensation builds up inside the boxes with predictable results. Store cables, spare fuses, or tools in there. Not radios.

Cable Routing

Routing coax, power cables, and control wiring through a vehicle requires patience and planning. Here are the essentials:

Route coax and power cables separately where possible. When they must share a path, keep power cables at least a few inches from coax. Use existing grommets and wire passages wherever the vehicle provides them — the factory engineers have already identified the safe routes through the firewall and body panels. If you must drill a new hole, deburr it thoroughly and install a proper grommet. A coax cable chafing against a raw metal edge will eventually short, and you will spend hours troubleshooting a problem that was entirely preventable.

Keep cables away from moving parts: seat rails, pedal mechanisms, steering columns, and door hinges. Secure cables with proper clamps or tie wraps at regular intervals. A cable that hangs loose will eventually find something to snag on, and it is always the most inaccessible cable in the installation.

The Bottom Line

A good mobile installation is not glamorous work. It requires crawling under dashboards, pulling headliners, running cables through tight spaces, and drilling holes in a vehicle you probably still owe money on. It takes a repair manual, the right tools, a free weekend, and the willingness to do things correctly even when nobody will see the result.

The reward is a station that works reliably, sounds good on the air, does not generate interference, and will not try to kill you in a collision. That seems like a reasonable trade for a few drilled holes.

Modern Vehicle Installation Challenges

Everything I have discussed above assumes a vehicle that was designed with at least a passing nod to the possibility that someone might want to modify it. That era is ending. Vehicles built in the last few years present a set of challenges that did not exist a decade ago, and they are only getting worse. If you are shopping for a vehicle and you intend to install HF radio equipment, you need to understand what you are getting into before you sign the paperwork.

Touchscreen Infotainment Systems

The days of pulling out a factory head unit and replacing it with an aftermarket unit are effectively over. In most modern vehicles, the infotainment screen is not just a radio — it is the control interface for the HVAC system, the backup camera, navigation, vehicle settings, and increasingly, the Advanced Driver Assistance Systems (ADAS). Remove it and you lose half the car's functionality. There is no DIN-sized slot behind it waiting for your Pioneer. It is integrated, and it is staying.

This matters to us because RF energy in the vicinity of these touchscreens causes problems. Phantom touches. Screen freezes. Spontaneous reboots. The capacitive touch sensors in modern displays are sensitive to RF fields, and running 100 watts of HF through a coax cable that passes directly behind the dashboard is a fine way to demonstrate this. I have seen installations where keying the transmitter on 20 meters reliably triggered the climate control to switch to maximum defrost. Amusing the first time; less so in January.

💡 TIP

If your coax must route behind or near the dashboard area, install a common-mode choke on the coax before it enters that space. A half-dozen turns of coax through a suitable mix 31 toroid will often eliminate touchscreen interference. Address this during installation — not after the headliner is back in place and the trim panels are reinstalled.

Panoramic Glass Roofs

Panoramic glass roofs — those expansive, gorgeous, light-flooding sheets of glass that cover most or all of the roof — are becoming standard equipment on an alarming number of vehicles. They are wonderful for stargazing. They are an unmitigated disaster for HF mobile operation.

⚠ WARNING

A panoramic glass roof is an unmitigated disaster for HF mobile operators. There is no ground plane where there is no metal. No amount of clever engineering, bonding straps, or wishful thinking will turn a glass roof into a functional antenna counterpoise. If you are serious about HF mobile, think very carefully before buying a vehicle with a full panoramic roof.

Glass is not a ground plane. It does not matter that the glass has a thin metallic UV-reflective coating — those coatings are measured in nanometers and are electrically irrelevant at RF. You cannot mount an NMO in glass. You cannot create a ground plane where there is no metal. This is not a problem you can engineer around with enough creativity. It is physics.

If you already own such a vehicle, your options are limited to finding whatever metal remains: the roof rail structures (if they are steel and not aluminum extrusions bonded with adhesive), the short metal section at the rear of the roof behind the glass, or the trunk lid. None of these are ideal. All of them are compromises. But a compromise mounted on actual metal will outperform a miracle solution mounted on glass every single time.

🔑 KEY CONCEPT

Before purchasing any vehicle for mobile HF use, verify exactly how much metal roof area remains. Open the headliner photos in the owner forums. Check for a steel roof panel versus a full glass panel. A vehicle with 18 inches of metal at the rear of the roof is workable. A vehicle with nothing but glass from windshield to tailgate is not. This is a buying decision, not an installation decision.

Modern Trim Panel Removal

Beyond the antenna mounting surface itself, the challenge of routing cables and hardware through modern vehicle interiors has changed considerably. Older vehicles used screws to hold interior trim panels in place. You could see the screws — or at least find them under obvious covers — remove them, pull the panel, do your work, and put the panel back. Simple.

Modern vehicles use plastic push clips, and those clips are designed for exactly one installation. Pull the panel and the clips break. Some of them. Sometimes most of them. Now your trim panel does not fit snugly anymore, and it rattles at highway speed, and you have created a problem that did not exist before you started. The solution is straightforward: order replacement clips before you begin the installation. Your vehicle's parts department or a supplier like Dorman can provide them. They cost pennies. Buy twice as many as you think you need.

Modern panels also contain things that older panels did not. Ambient LED lighting strips. Speaker assemblies. Proximity sensors. Wiring harnesses with connectors you have never seen before. Pulling a door panel without knowing what is behind it — and what is connected to it — is a recipe for broken connectors and unexplained warning lights on the dashboard.

💡 TIP

Before pulling any trim panel: photograph it from multiple angles, including every visible connector and wire routing. Use plastic pry tools — never metal screwdrivers — to release clips. Work slowly and methodically. Budget at least twice the time you think the job will take. If a clip resists, there is a reason, and brute force is not the answer.

Adhesive-Bonded Body Panels

Here is something that catches even experienced installers off guard. Many modern vehicles use structural adhesive to bond body panels to the frame and to each other. This is standard practice in the automotive industry now — it reduces weight, improves rigidity, and eliminates the corrosion problems associated with spot welds on dissimilar metals.

The problem for us is that structural adhesive is an insulator. A metal body panel that is bonded to the vehicle structure with adhesive may be electrically floating — completely isolated from the vehicle chassis at RF frequencies. It looks like metal. You can tap on it and it sounds like metal. But your multimeter will tell you it has no electrical connection to the rest of the vehicle.

Before you mount an antenna on any body panel, verify continuity between that panel and the vehicle chassis with a multimeter. If there is no continuity, you need a bonding strap — a short, wide piece of copper braid connecting the panel to a known chassis ground point, with paint removed at both ends and proper hardware securing it. Without this step, you are mounting your antenna on an isolated chunk of metal that is not part of the ground plane, and your antenna performance will reflect that fact.

Heated Windshields

Heated windshields have been around for years — Ford's Quickclear system is the most well-known, but VW, BMW, and others offer them as well. These windshields contain a conductive layer embedded in the glass that serves as a resistive heating element. Run current through it and it melts ice. Simple enough in concept.

The conductive layer is a problem for two reasons. First, it distorts VHF and UHF antenna patterns. If you mount an antenna on or immediately adjacent to a heated windshield, the conductive layer acts as a parasitic element, skewing and attenuating your signal in unpredictable ways. Second, the heating controller itself — the electronics that switch high current through that conductive layer — is a broadband RFI source. It generates noise across a wide frequency range, and it is located right there in the dashboard area where your receiver is trying to hear weak signals.

⚠ WARNING

Do not mount VHF/UHF antennas on or adjacent to a heated windshield. The conductive heating layer will distort antenna patterns and the heating controller will generate broadband RFI. Mount antennas on the roof or trunk, well clear of the heated glass.

Factory Dash Cameras and ADAS Sensors

Tesla vehicles have eight or more cameras. Rivian uses eleven. GM's newer vehicles are laden with cameras and sensors for their hands-free driving systems. These are not aftermarket dash cams that you can unplug — they are integral to the vehicle's Advanced Driver Assistance Systems. They share processors, wiring harnesses, and control modules with features like automatic emergency braking, lane keeping, and adaptive cruise control. Disconnecting them is not an option unless you enjoy warning lights, degraded vehicle functionality, and potentially voided warranties.

Every one of these cameras and sensors is a broadband digital noise source. High-speed digital data — video streams, sensor data, processing signals — radiates RF noise across a wide spectrum. And these devices are distributed around the entire perimeter of the vehicle: front bumper, rear bumper, fenders, mirrors, windshield header, tailgate. You are surrounded by digital noise generators, and there is nothing you can do about it except understand that they exist, identify the specific frequencies they affect, and work around them where possible.

💡 TIP

If your modern vehicle has a higher-than-expected noise floor on certain bands, factory cameras and ADAS sensors are a likely culprit. Systematic noise hunting with the engine running and all systems active — as described in the RFI article — is the only way to identify and characterize these sources.
