Safety

Last updated: March 2026
Based on original work by Alan Applegate, K0BG Preserved & expanded for the amateur radio community
⚠ Safety Notice

This article discusses airbag (SRS) systems, high-voltage RF, vehicle electrical systems, and lithium battery hazards. The procedures and warnings described here are educational. Always consult your vehicle's service manual and a qualified automotive professional before working near SRS components. Improper work near airbag systems can cause accidental deployment resulting in serious injury or death.

In This Article

Safety is not a suggestion. It is not something you get around to after the installation is working. It is the first consideration and the last check, and if you treat it as anything less, you are putting yourself, your passengers, and other drivers at risk. I have seen hams do spectacularly dangerous things in the name of getting on the air from their vehicles, and the fact that most of them got away with it does not make it acceptable.

This article covers every major safety consideration in a mobile amateur radio installation. Read it before you start. Refer back to it during your installation. And if something here conflicts with your plan, change your plan — not this article.

Airbags and the SRS System

Modern vehicles are packed with airbags, and most hams have no idea how many or where they are located. The Supplemental Restraint System (SRS) in a current-production vehicle typically includes:

  • Steering wheel airbag — directly in front of the driver
  • Passenger dashboard airbag — concealed behind the dash surface on the passenger side
  • Side curtain airbags — hidden along the headliner, deploying downward to cover the side windows
  • Seat-mounted side airbags — in the outboard bolsters of the front seats
  • Knee airbags — below the steering column and sometimes the passenger side

The wiring for these systems runs through the roof pillars (A-pillar, B-pillar, and C-pillar), along the headliner, under the dash, and through the seats. When you route your radio wiring or coax through any of these areas, you risk damaging SRS wiring. If an airbag fails to deploy in a collision because you nicked its wiring while fishing coax behind the headliner, someone may die.

⚠ WARNING

NEVER work near SRS wiring without first disconnecting the vehicle battery and waiting a minimum of 10 minutes. The SRS system contains capacitors that store enough energy to deploy an airbag even with the battery disconnected. These capacitors require time to discharge. Your vehicle's service manual will specify the exact wait time — for some vehicles it is 10 minutes, for others it is longer. Do not guess. An airbag deploying in your face while you are working under the dash can cause severe injury or death.

SRS wiring is typically enclosed in bright yellow conduit or has yellow connectors. Learn to recognize it. Do not cut it, splice into it, zip-tie your wiring to it, or run your cables in the same conduit. Give it a wide berth and treat it with the respect that an explosive device deserves — because that is exactly what an airbag inflator is.

Dashboard Mounting and Airbag Deployment Zones

Do not mount anything atop the dashboard. This bears repeating from the ABCs article because it is a safety issue, not merely an installation preference. The passenger airbag deploys upward through a concealed seam in the dash surface. Anything sitting on that surface — a radio head, a GPS, a cell phone mount, your coffee cup — becomes a projectile. At the speeds involved in airbag deployment, even a small object can cause lethal injuries.

Do not mount equipment in foot wells, particularly the driver's. An object that slides under the brake pedal during a panic stop or collision will prevent you from stopping the vehicle. It does not matter how small it is or how well you believe it is secured.

RF Exposure

Radio frequency energy at the power levels we use in mobile operation is not ionizing radiation, and it will not give you cancer. But it can cause tissue heating, and at HF through UHF frequencies, the effects are well documented and real. The FCC publishes guidelines in OET Bulletin 65 that every amateur is required to follow. Being in a vehicle does not exempt you.

Antenna Placement

The single most important factor in RF exposure safety is keeping the antenna as far from the passenger compartment as possible. A center-of-roof mount is ideal for VHF/UHF. For HF whip antennas, a rear bumper, trailer hitch, or rear fender mount puts the maximum practical distance between the antenna and the vehicle occupants.

Power density decreases with the square of the distance from the antenna. Doubling the distance reduces exposure by a factor of four. This is why a trunk-lip mount at the rear of the vehicle is dramatically safer than a fender mount near the passenger compartment.

HF vs. VHF Exposure Considerations

The human body absorbs RF energy differently at different frequencies. At HF frequencies (3–30 MHz), the wavelengths are long relative to the human body, and absorption is relatively low but not zero. At VHF and especially UHF frequencies (above 30 MHz), the body absorbs energy much more efficiently. A 50-watt VHF signal from a poorly placed antenna is a greater exposure concern than 100 watts on 80 meters from the same distance.

Do not operate high power (more than 50 watts) on VHF/UHF with the antenna mounted anywhere close to the passenger compartment. And never transmit on any frequency with a handheld held against your head inside a vehicle — the metal roof and pillars create reflections that can increase local RF exposure significantly.

💡 TIP

Perform an RF exposure evaluation for your specific installation using the FCC's OET Bulletin 65 guidelines or the ARRL's online RF exposure calculator. This is not optional — it is a regulatory requirement. Document your results and keep them with your station records.

Electrical Safety

Improperly wired radio installations cause vehicle fires. This is not an exaggeration or a scare tactic. I have personally seen the aftermath of three vehicle fires caused by amateur radio wiring, and I have heard of dozens more. In every case, the cause was the same: inadequate fusing, undersized wire, or both.

Fusing

Always fuse at the battery. Both the positive and negative leads must have fuses, and those fuses must be located as close to the battery terminals as possible. The purpose of a fuse is not to protect your radio — it is to protect the wire. If the wire chafes through its insulation and contacts the vehicle body (which is the negative return path), the full current capacity of your battery will flow through that wire. A car battery can deliver 500 to 1,000 amperes into a dead short. An unfused 10-gauge wire carrying that current will glow red and ignite anything it touches in seconds.

🔑 KEY CONCEPT

The fuse protects the wire, not the radio. Size the fuse for the wire gauge, not for the equipment. A 40-amp fuse on a wire rated for 20 amps is not a fuse — it is a fire waiting to happen. Both the positive and negative leads must be dedicated runs from the battery with fuses at the battery end.

Dedicated Wiring

Never use existing vehicle wiring to power radio equipment. Never tap into the fuse box. Never splice into the cigarette lighter circuit. Every one of these shortcuts introduces noise into your receiver, creates ground loops, and risks overloading circuits that were designed for specific loads. Run dedicated wires directly from the battery, properly fused, using wire gauges appropriate for the current draw and the length of the run. See the Wiring article for sizing tables and routing guidance.

Driving Distraction

Let me be direct: operating a radio while driving is an inherently distracting activity. You are splitting your attention between piloting a multi-ton vehicle at highway speeds and communicating on the radio. The only responsible way to manage this is to minimize the operational demands of the radio to the greatest extent possible.

🔑 THE NUMBERS

Distracted driving is the leading cause of motor vehicle crashes and deaths. Driver distraction is statistically 7 times more deadly than drunk driving. In two seconds of distraction at 60 mph, your vehicle travels 176 feet — approximately 10 car lengths, or about the distance a loaded SUV takes to stop from 60 mph. Every single device you install in a vehicle is also one more thing you need to look at while you're supposed to be watching the road. That cost is real every time you reach for a control.

Automation

Use an automatic antenna controller. Use a radio with memories programmed for your most-used frequencies. Use voice keying if you operate contests. Use anything and everything that reduces the number of times you must look away from the road or take a hand off the wheel.

Do not tune your antenna while driving. Do not adjust your screwdriver antenna manually while driving. Do not swap resonators while driving. If you must make changes that require your attention, pull over. It will cost you two minutes. An accident will cost you a great deal more.

⚠ WARNING

Many jurisdictions now have distracted driving laws that explicitly or implicitly cover amateur radio operation. A radio mounted where it obstructs your view, or the act of operating it in a way that diverts your attention, can result in citations. More importantly, it can result in collisions. No QSO is worth a life.

Lightning

A vertical antenna on the roof of your vehicle is, by definition, the highest point on the vehicle. Lightning will find it. While a direct strike on a moving vehicle is relatively rare, it does happen, and the consequences range from destroyed electronics to structural damage to the vehicle.

DC-grounded antennas — those with a direct DC path from the antenna element to the vehicle body — are inherently safer than antennas with series capacitors or matching networks that block DC. A DC-grounded antenna provides a path for static charge to dissipate before it builds to the point where a strike becomes likely. Most screwdriver antennas and continuously loaded whips are DC grounded. Many base-loaded antennas are not.

If you are parked during a thunderstorm, disconnect the coax from the radio. Better yet, disconnect the antenna entirely if practical. The energy in even a nearby strike can induce voltages in your antenna system that will destroy your transceiver's front end. A replacement radio costs far more than the inconvenience of disconnecting a cable.

High Voltage Hazards

Amplifiers

Mobile amplifiers that use vacuum tubes (and some solid-state designs with internal power supplies) contain lethal voltages. A typical mobile tube amplifier operates with plate voltages of 1,200 to 2,500 volts DC. This will kill you. It will not merely give you a tingle or throw you across the room — it will stop your heart.

Never open a mobile amplifier enclosure while it is connected to power. Even after disconnecting power, capacitors in the power supply may retain a lethal charge for minutes. If you are not trained in high-voltage safety procedures, do not service your own amplifier. Send it to someone who is.

Antenna Tip Voltages

Loading coils on HF mobile antennas can develop surprisingly high RF voltages at the tip of the antenna during transmission. On the lower bands (160 and 80 meters), where the antenna is a tiny fraction of a wavelength and the loading coil has high Q, tip voltages can exceed several thousand volts. This is enough to cause painful RF burns on contact and can arc to nearby grounded objects.

Keep this in mind when choosing an antenna length and mounting location. An antenna tip that a pedestrian, cyclist, or occupant of an adjacent vehicle can touch is a liability. Antennas mounted low on the vehicle body are particularly problematic in this regard. A whip mounted at bumper height with its tip at face level for a pedestrian is an RF burn waiting to happen.

Vehicle-Specific Dangers

Hybrid and Electric Vehicle High-Voltage Systems

Hybrid and electric vehicles contain high-voltage systems operating at 300 to 800 volts DC. The orange-jacketed cables running between the battery pack, inverter, and motor carry enough energy to kill you instantly. These cables are routed through areas of the vehicle where you might be inclined to run radio wiring — under the rear seats, through the floor pan, along the center tunnel.

Do not disturb, reroute, or run your wiring alongside the high-voltage cables in a hybrid or EV. Consult your service manual for the exact routing of all high-voltage components before you plan any wiring routes. Some hybrid vehicles also have high-voltage junction boxes under seats or in the trunk area where you might otherwise mount a transceiver body.

Fuel Line Routing

Know where your fuel lines run before you route any wiring. Fuel lines in most vehicles run along the floor pan, often on the driver's side, from the tank at the rear to the engine compartment. A chafed wire in contact with a fuel line is a catastrophic failure waiting to happen. Route your radio wiring on the opposite side of the vehicle from the fuel lines whenever possible, and never secure radio wiring to fuel lines or their mounting brackets.

🔑 KEY CONCEPT

Your vehicle's service manual is the definitive reference for the location of every safety-critical system — airbags, fuel lines, high-voltage cables, brake lines, and structural members. Buy one before you start your installation. There is no substitute and no shortcut.

A Final Word

Every safety precaution in this article adds time and effort to your installation. None of them add to the fun of operating mobile. And every single one of them exists because someone, somewhere, learned the hard way that it was necessary. Some of those people lived to tell the story. Some did not.

Mobile amateur radio is a wonderful capability. It connects you to the world while you travel, provides communications in emergencies, and makes long drives far more enjoyable. But it must be done responsibly. Take the time to do it safely, and you will enjoy it for decades. Cut corners, and you may not enjoy anything at all.

Modern Vehicle Safety Considerations

Everything above remains as true as the day it was written, but vehicles have not stood still. The 2020s brought a raft of new systems that directly affect how — and where — you can safely install amateur radio equipment. If you are working on a vehicle built after roughly 2018, the following sections are not optional reading. They are the difference between a professional installation and a trip to the dealer with a five-figure repair bill, or worse.

ADAS Sensors and Cameras

Advanced Driver Assistance Systems — ADAS — have proliferated to the point where even base-model trucks and economy cars are bristling with sensors. Expect to find 77 GHz millimeter-wave radar behind the front grille and in the rear bumper, cameras embedded in the windshield header, side mirrors, tailgate handle, and B-pillars, and ultrasonic proximity sensors ringing the vehicle at bumper height. These systems control adaptive cruise control, automatic emergency braking, lane-keeping assist, blind-spot monitoring, and parking assist. They are not convenience features. They are safety systems your vehicle relies on.

The problem for mobile hams is straightforward: the wiring for these sensors runs through the same areas we have traditionally used for coax and power cable routing. Camera harnesses follow the headliner and A-pillars. Radar module wiring runs behind grilles and through bumper covers. Ultrasonic wiring traces the perimeter of the vehicle at exactly the height where you might be drilling for an NMO mount.

⚠ WARNING

Disturbing an ADAS sensor's position by even a fraction of a degree will invalidate its calibration. Removing an interior panel and reinstalling it slightly differently, bumping a radar module, or flexing a windshield camera bracket during coax routing can all cause miscalibration. Recalibration requires dealer-level scan tools and targets, and typically costs $200 to $500 per sensor (as of 2024). Some require a static calibration bay that only dealers and specialized shops have. Get your vehicle's service manual, learn where every sensor and its wiring live, and stay well clear.

More Airbags Than Ever

The airbag section earlier in this article covers the basics, but vehicles built since 2020 have taken the concept considerably further. Far-side airbags now deploy from the inboard edge of front seats to prevent occupant-to-occupant contact in side impacts. Center airbags deploy between the front seats. Rear-seat side curtain airbags protect second-row occupants. And — the one that catches the most hams off guard — seatbelt pretensioner pyrotechnics are now routinely located in the B-pillars.

The B-pillar has long been a favored coax routing path. It is a straight shot from the headliner to the rocker panel, it is easy to access by removing trim panels, and it avoids the complexity of the A-pillar and dash area. It is also now packed with pyrotechnic devices. Pretensioner charges, which fire to cinch seatbelts tight in a collision, use the same type of electrically initiated explosive squibs as airbag inflators. Their wiring follows the same yellow-connector convention — but not always. Some manufacturers use different colors for pretensioner circuits.

⚠ WARNING

Treat seatbelt pretensioner wiring with exactly the same caution as airbag wiring. The same rules apply: disconnect the battery, wait the specified discharge time, identify all SRS wiring before you touch anything, and never run your cables alongside or zip-tied to pretensioner harnesses. A pretensioner firing while you are routing coax through the B-pillar will, at minimum, ruin your day. Consult your service manual — the number and location of pyrotechnic devices in modern B-pillars may surprise you.

48V Mild Hybrid Systems

Here is one that catches people because they do not know they have it. Since 2019, a growing number of vehicles use 48-volt mild hybrid systems: the Ram 1500 eTorque, numerous Mercedes-Benz models with EQ Boost, many Audis, some BMWs, and the list grows every model year. These systems use a belt-driven starter-generator and a small lithium battery pack to provide stop-start capability, electric boost during acceleration, and energy recovery during braking. They are not full hybrids. The vehicle does not drive on electric power alone. And that is precisely why owners often have no idea the system exists.

The 48V wiring in these systems is identified by blue cable jacketing — not the orange associated with full high-voltage hybrids and EVs. It is technically below the 60-volt threshold that defines “high voltage” in automotive standards. Do not let that mislead you into complacency.

💡 TIP

48 volts will not electrocute you, but it will arc-weld a wrench to a terminal and start a fire before you can react. A 48V lithium battery pack can deliver enormous short-circuit currents. If you short a 48V bus with a tool, the arc is sustained and hot enough to ignite insulation, upholstery, and anything else nearby. Know whether your vehicle has a 48V system — check for blue-jacketed cables and a secondary battery (often under the rear seat or in the trunk). Treat the 48V bus with the same respect you give 12V battery cables: fuse everything, insulate everything, and do not create short circuits.

Lithium Battery Hazards

This applies to full hybrids, EVs, 48V mild hybrids, and increasingly to the auxiliary 12V batteries that some manufacturers are replacing with lithium units. Lithium batteries are not oversized versions of the lead-acid battery you grew up with. They are a fundamentally different technology with a fundamentally different failure mode, and if you do not understand that difference, you are not prepared to work around them.

When a lithium cell fails — from physical damage, a short circuit, overcharging, or manufacturing defect — it can enter thermal runaway. The cell temperature escalates uncontrollably, reaching over 1,000°F. The resulting fire produces toxic hydrogen fluoride gas, which is immediately dangerous to life. You cannot extinguish a lithium battery fire with a conventional fire extinguisher. Water slows the propagation but does not stop the electrochemical reaction. And a lithium battery that appears to be extinguished can re-ignite minutes or hours later as adjacent cells reach their thermal runaway threshold.

⚠ WARNING

Know what type of battery your vehicle has before you start any installation work. If your vehicle uses lithium batteries of any kind — traction pack, 48V auxiliary, or 12V starter — understand that a puncture, crush, or short circuit of that battery is a life-threatening emergency, not merely an inconvenience. Do not drill, cut, or route wiring near lithium battery packs without consulting the service manual for exclusion zones. If you damage a lithium cell, evacuate the vehicle and call the fire department. Do not attempt to fight the fire yourself.

Updated FCC RF Exposure Rules

This one is not about vehicle safety, but it is about regulatory compliance that directly affects every mobile installation, and most hams I talk to are not yet aware of the change.

🔑 KEY CONCEPT

In May 2023, updated FCC rules from ET Docket 19-226 took effect that removed the categorical exemptions many amateurs had relied on to avoid performing RF exposure evaluations. Under the previous rules, most amateur stations below certain power thresholds were categorically exempt from routine evaluation. That exemption is gone. Routine evaluation is now mandatory for most amateur installations, including mobile stations. The ARRL has updated their online RF exposure calculator to reflect the new rules. Perform the evaluation for your specific installation — antenna type, power level, mounting location, and distance to occupied areas — and document the results. Keep the documentation with your station records. The rules require it, and in the event of an RF exposure complaint, your documentation is your defense.

Pedestrian Airbags and Active Hood Hinges

If you route wiring through the engine bay or anywhere near the hood hinge area, you need to know whether your vehicle has a pedestrian protection system. Volvo has used pedestrian airbags — which deploy from under the rear edge of the hood to cushion a struck pedestrian's impact with the windshield — since 2012. Subaru, Jaguar, Land Rover, and others use active hood hinges with pyrotechnic actuators that pop the rear of the hood upward on impact to create a crush space between the hood and the engine below.

These pyrotechnic actuators are located in the hood hinge area, and their wiring runs along the firewall and fender wells. If you are routing power cables or coax through the engine bay — which you may well be doing if your antenna mount is on the fender or near the hood — you must know where these devices and their wiring are located. An active hood hinge is an explosive actuator. Treat it with the same respect as any other pyrotechnic safety device in the vehicle: identify it, identify its wiring, and stay away from both.

Headsets, VOX, and Speech Compression

Headsets can be useful for mobile operation, but their use raises important legal and operational considerations. In many jurisdictions, the use of a headset by the driver is illegal or limited. Know your local laws before you put on a headset in the car.

⚠ NEVER USE A TWO-EARPIECE HEADSET WHILE DRIVING

Never use a two-earpiece headset (or full earphones) while driving — for very obvious reasons, legal or not. You must be able to hear the road: emergency vehicles, horns, and tire sounds that warn of problems. A single-earpiece headset is the maximum. If you are not sure of the law in your area, don't wear any headset at all. The radio can wait.

The use of VOX while mobile-in-motion is not recommended. If you do use VOX, a headset with a noise-canceling microphone is a prerequisite. Set anti-vox to its lowest setting, and turn off the speaker. In a relatively noisy vehicle, VOX will misfire frequently — it keys up on road noise, your passengers, and anything else above the threshold.

Speech compression should not be used when operating mobile. It increases average power out and therefore current draw, which overtaxes the vehicle's electrical system — especially in cold, wet weather. It also brings up the background ambient noise level during lulls in speech, which can be very annoying. Low mic gain and no compression is the correct operating setup for mobile use.

💡 TIP: MONITOR YOUR OWN AUDIO

Within the confines of an average vehicle, the ambient noise level is many times higher than your average living room. To compensate, operators often tend to shout — which sounds awful and raises the noise floor of your transmitted signal. Turn on your transceiver's monitor function and adjust the level so you can just barely hear it in the background. This instant sidetone feedback will typically keep you on an even keel without having to consciously think about microphone technique.

Operating in Rain and Wet Weather

Rain raises several antenna safety and performance issues that are worth understanding before you find out the hard way.

Hustler Antennas and Rain

⚠ WARNING

Hustler antennas and moisture do not get along. The vinyl heat-shrink tubing covering the loading coil allows moisture to seep underneath. Once moisture is trapped under that covering and you continue to transmit, the coil arcs internally. A coil that has arced is destroyed — it becomes a dummy load. If you use Hustler antennas, apply a coat of Rain-X to the coil covering before the wet season and reapply periodically. Open bug-catcher coils are only marginally better: keep them clean and dry, and Rain-X them too. High power levels combined with moisture in a loading coil will produce arc-over on even a coil that appears to be in good condition.

RF Tracking and Insulators in Rain

Rain can cause RF tracking problems on antenna mounting hardware and insulators. When moisture bridges across a surface that has been treated with car wax or polish, the problem is significantly worse — most car wax formulations contain conductive agents added specifically to control dust build-up. Ironically, waxing your antenna mount area makes it more likely to fail in rain. Rain-X on mounting hardware and insulators is a far better choice. Even then, very high power levels can cause insulator breakdown in wet conditions.

Slip-Stream Static

Slip-stream static produces a sustained, high-level hash on receive, most noticeable at higher frequencies. It is caused by aerodynamic charging of the antenna element and vehicle surface as air flows past. Unlike rain static — which is a precursor to lightning and comes in bursts — slip-stream static is continuous at highway speeds and varies with vehicle speed. A static drain (a small resistance, typically 1–10 megohms, from the antenna element to ground) along with proper DC grounding of the antenna mount, eliminates the problem in most cases. If you experience steady HF hash that disappears when you stop the vehicle, slip-stream static is the likely cause.
