Antenna Controllers

Last updated: March 2026
Based on original work by Alan Applegate, K0BG Preserved & expanded for the amateur radio community
In This Article

What Controllers Do (and Don't Do)

An antenna controller automates the process of adjusting a remotely tuned mobile HF antenna. Instead of stopping the car, getting out, and manually turning the adjustment mechanism on your antenna coil, you press a button (or let the radio do it for you) and the controller drives a motor that changes the resonant frequency. That is the promise, anyway. The reality is considerably more nuanced, and the path from "plug it in" to "reliable automatic operation" is littered with the carcasses of installations done by people who did not understand what they were dealing with.

There are two fundamental types of antenna controllers: turn-counting and SWR-sensing. Each works on a completely different principle, each has distinct advantages and pitfalls, and confusing the requirements of one with the other is a reliable recipe for frustration.

Turn-Counting Controllers

The turn-counting controller is conceptually simple. A small magnet is attached to the motor shaft (or gear train) inside the antenna. Each time the shaft completes a rotation, the magnet passes a reed switch and generates a pulse. The controller counts these pulses.

To set up the system, you "park" the antenna — collapsing the coil to its highest-frequency position (shortest electrical length). From that known starting point, you tune each band manually, noting the pulse count for each resonant point. These counts are stored in the controller's memory. From then on, when you want to change bands, you press a button and the controller drives the motor until the pulse count matches the stored value for that band.

The MFJ-1924 and Ameritron SDC-102B are common examples of turn-counting controllers. Some models interface with the radio's data port (CI-V or equivalent), allowing the controller to automatically select the correct antenna position when you change frequency on the radio. In theory, this is the hands-free mobile operating experience everyone wants.

A Word About Parking

"Parking" means driving the antenna coil to one extreme of its travel — typically the highest-frequency (most collapsed) position. This establishes the known reference point from which all pulse counts are measured. Every time the controller loses its position reference (power interruption, miscounted pulse, etc.), it must re-park to recalibrate.

💡 TIP

Some controllers, including newer versions of the MFJ-1924, auto-park and recalibrate every time they power up. While this ensures accuracy, it adds mechanical wear to the antenna's motor and gear train. Every park cycle runs the motor to the end of its travel, and those cycles add up over time. If you leave your radio on all day and the controller re-parks every time, you are putting unnecessary miles on a mechanism that was not designed for infinite duty cycles.

⚠ WARNING

Cold climates and memory-backed controllers: Many turn-counting controllers use a small lithium cell or supercapacitor to maintain stored band positions when the vehicle is off. In cold climates, these backup power sources lose capacity rapidly. If you operate in sustained cold weather and find your stored band positions disappearing between operating sessions, the controller's memory backup is failing. Replace the battery or supercapacitor, or adopt the habit of re-parking and re-entering positions each outing. This is not a controller malfunction — it is a well-understood behavior of small energy storage devices at low temperatures.

SDC-100 Erratic Operation

The SDC-100 (predecessor to the SDC-102B) is known for erratic behavior that sends operators on wild goose chases through their entire installation. Before you tear apart your wiring or blame RFI, check the stall current setting. The SDC-100 detects the end of antenna travel by sensing when motor current spikes (because the motor has stalled against the mechanical stop). If the stall current threshold is set incorrectly, the controller either overshoots (does not detect the stall) or stops prematurely (false stall detection from normal running current variation). It is a simple adjustment that solves an outsized percentage of SDC-100 complaints.

SWR-Sensing Controllers

SWR-sensing controllers take a different approach. Instead of counting motor turns, they transmit a brief carrier, measure the SWR, and drive the motor in the direction that lowers the SWR. The process repeats until the SWR falls below a preset threshold. No parking, no pulse counting, no stored positions. The controller simply hunts for the lowest SWR in real time.

This sounds like the superior approach, and in some ways it is. There is no reference point to lose, no recalibration needed, and the system adapts automatically to any frequency you transmit on. But there is a critical prerequisite that many operators overlook, and overlooking it is the single most common reason SWR-sensing controllers fail to perform.

🔑 KEY CONCEPT

Proper impedance matching is an absolute prerequisite for reliable SWR-sensing controller operation. The controller can only find resonance by minimizing SWR. If the antenna system is not properly matched to 50 ohms at resonance, the SWR minimum the controller finds may not correspond to actual resonance. The controller will park the antenna at whatever point produces the lowest SWR number, which — as explained in the Antenna Matching article — is frequently not the point of true resonance.

For true automatic operation with an SWR-sensing controller, a fixed shunt coil is the only correct matching solution. No substitutes — not a UNUN, not a capacitance box, not an autotransformer. The shunt coil must be properly sized for your antenna and mounting configuration. Without proper matching, the SWR-sensing controller is chasing a phantom. It will find a minimum, but that minimum may not be where you want the antenna tuned.

Choke Placement: The Make-or-Break Detail

If there is one thing in this entire article that you remember, make it this: the motor lead choke must be located outside the vehicle, as close to the antenna base as physically possible.

Not inside the vehicle. Not under the dashboard. Not in the trunk, tucked in next to the controller. Outside. At the antenna base.

⚠ WARNING

If the motor lead choke is inside the vehicle, you will be plagued with RFI and erratic controller operation. This is not a maybe. This is not a sometimes. This is a certainty. Everything on the antenna side of the choke is part of the antenna system, coupled to the RF field. Motor leads running from the antenna base, through the vehicle body, to a choke inside the cab are acting as antenna elements themselves, radiating and receiving RF energy the entire length of their run. The controller is connected directly to these RF-carrying wires, and it will behave accordingly — which is to say, badly.

The reason is fundamental. The motor control wires run through the RF field of the antenna. Without adequate choking, RF current flows on those wires right back to the controller. The controller's electronics were not designed to operate in the presence of significant RF energy. The reed switch signals get corrupted. The motor drive signals get distorted. The controller makes wrong decisions, misses counts, and generally acts possessed.

Placing the choke at the antenna base, outside the vehicle, means the RF energy is blocked before it ever enters the vehicle. The wires from the choke to the controller inside the car are clean, carrying only DC motor current and reed switch pulses. The controller operates in a quiet electrical environment and works as intended.

Factory-Supplied Chokes Are Inadequate

I will say this plainly: the ferrite beads and toroids shipped with most antenna controllers and antenna kits are almost always inadequate. They are too small. They use the wrong ferrite mix. They have insufficient turns. They are, to put it charitably, a token gesture toward a problem that demands a serious solution.

The choke impedance must be at least two orders of magnitude greater than the circuit impedance it is protecting. In practical terms, this means the choke must present at least 5,000 ohms (5 kilohms) of impedance at the operating frequency. For short or stubby antennas, or installations with marginal mounting, the requirement goes up — 10 to 15 kilohms is not unreasonable.

🔥 MYTH BUSTED

"The antenna came with ferrite beads for the motor leads, so I'm covered." No, you are not. Those beads typically provide a few hundred ohms of impedance at best. That is one to two orders of magnitude below what is needed. You need a proper wound choke on the correct ferrite material, with enough turns to develop the required impedance. The factory beads are better than nothing, but "better than nothing" is a low bar.

KEY SPECS: Mix 31 Split Beads

Mix 31 split beads are ideal for motor lead chokes. However, it takes 8 turns through a 3/4-inch ID mix 31 bead to reach 5kΩ of impedance. That means you must use the 3/4-inch ID size — the smaller ID split beads simply do not provide enough winding room to achieve adequate impedance. Mix 31 beads in this size are available from DX Engineering and other dealers; a package of five costs approximately $35. Do not assume you can substitute a different ferrite mix or use fewer turns. You cannot.

Why do short or stubby antennas require more choke impedance? Because shorter antennas have lower radiation resistance, which means the currents in the system are proportionally higher for a given power level. Higher current means more RF on the motor leads, which means you need a higher-impedance choke to keep those currents out of the controller. The math is straightforward, even if the antenna manufacturers prefer to ignore it.

Critical Incompatibility: IC-706 vs. IC-7000/7100/7200

WARNING: IC-706 Devices Will Damage the IC-7000 Series

The Icom IC-706 and IC-7000/7100/7200 differ fundamentally in how their tuner ports are configured. Devices that work correctly with the IC-706 may damage the IC-7000 series beyond repair. Here is why: many IC-706 devices place a resistor and capacitor between Pin 3 (13.8 VDC) and Pin 2 (TSTART) as a time constant to trigger a tuning carrier. On the IC-7000 series, this configuration stops the cooling fan from running, which guarantees the finals will fail. Additionally, Pin 1 (TKEY) is shared internally with the temperature control circuitry on the IC-7000 and must be left floating. Non-computerized devices used to mimic the AH-4 and trick the IC-706 into transmitting a 10-watt carrier are NOT compatible with the IC-7000 series. This includes suggested circuitry from SGC, at least three older screwdriver controller models, and most small tuner modules. Even if an old IC-706 unit appears to work correctly with a new IC-7000, do not use it.

Motor Leads Shorted to Mast

Here is a failure mode that will ruin your day and empty your wallet: if the motor control leads become shorted to the antenna mast — through chafing insulation, a pinched wire, or improper assembly — transmitting RF will send that energy directly into the controller. The result is not gradual degradation. The result is instantaneous destruction of the controller's output stage.

⚠ WARNING

If the motor leads are shorted to the antenna mast, transmitting will destroy the controller. Check for continuity between motor leads and the mast during installation and periodically thereafter. This is especially important after removing and reinstalling the antenna, when wires are most likely to get pinched or abraded.

A simple continuity check with a multimeter between each motor lead and the antenna mast will catch this before it becomes an expensive lesson. Do it during installation. Do it after any antenna service. It takes thirty seconds.

Radio Data Interface Issues

Many operators connect their antenna controller to the radio's data port (CI-V, CAT, or equivalent) so the controller automatically tracks frequency changes. This generally works well, but be aware of a compatibility issue: mail (email) software that uses the radio's data interface can cause conflicts.

Programs like Winlink, JS8Call, and various digital mode applications that poll the radio's frequency or send control commands through the data port may confuse the antenna controller. The controller sees frequency data on the bus, interprets it as a band change command, and starts driving the antenna motor. Or the controller's commands conflict with the software's commands, and both misbehave.

The solution is either to use separate data ports (if your radio has more than one) or to implement a switching arrangement that disconnects the controller from the data bus when digital software is active. Some operators simply unplug the controller's data cable when running digital modes. It is not elegant, but it works.

Choosing the Right Controller

The choice between turn-counting and SWR-sensing comes down to your operating style and how much effort you are willing to invest in the installation:

  • Turn-counting controllers are reliable and predictable once properly set up. They require initial calibration (parking and storing band positions) and periodic recalibration as temperature and other factors cause minor shifts. They work without transmitting, which means you can pre-position the antenna before you start calling. Their main weakness is that they can lose position reference, requiring a re-park.
  • SWR-sensing controllers are more automated in daily use — no stored positions, no parking. But they absolutely demand proper impedance matching to function correctly, and they require a brief transmit cycle to find resonance. If your matching is not right, the controller will never work reliably, and no amount of troubleshooting the controller itself will fix it.

Regardless of which type you choose, the installation fundamentals are the same: choke outside the vehicle at the antenna base, adequate choke impedance, proper impedance matching, and verified isolation of motor leads from the mast. Get these right and the controller will do its job. Skip any one of them and you will spend more time troubleshooting than operating.

The Bottom Line

Antenna controllers are not magic boxes. They are electromechanical devices that operate in a hostile RF environment, and they are only as good as the installation supporting them. The controller cannot compensate for poor bonding, inadequate chokes, missing impedance matching, or shorted motor leads. It can only do its narrowly defined job — counting turns or minimizing SWR — in an environment clean enough for its electronics to function.

Provide that environment and the controller becomes the reliable, convenient device you paid for. Fail to provide it and the controller becomes a source of endless frustration that you will eventually rip out and replace with manual tuning, cursing the manufacturer all the while for a problem that was never theirs to begin with.

The Controller Landscape Today

Availability

The mobile HF antenna controller market has always been thin. These are small-batch products from small companies serving a niche within a niche. Product lines appear, run for a few years, and quietly disappear when the one or two people who designed them move on to other things. That is the reality of this market, and it is unlikely to change.

Case in point: MFJ Enterprises, which manufactured the MFJ-1924 and its subsidiary Ameritron's SDC-102B, ceased all production in May 2024 after 52 years. These controllers are no longer manufactured new. Used units remain available through the usual channels, and remaining dealer stock may still be found, but plan accordingly.

The practical advice is simple: if you find a working controller — new or used — that does what you need, buy it. Do not wait for the next model. Do not hold out for a feature that might be added in a future revision. What was available six months ago may not be available today, and what is available today may not be available next month.

Smart Alternators and Supply Voltage

Modern vehicles no longer maintain a steady 13.8 or 14.2 volts at the battery. Smart alternators — also called variable-voltage or load-responsive alternators — modulate charging voltage anywhere from 12.5 to 15.5 volts depending on battery state of charge, electrical load, engine RPM, and even ambient temperature. The vehicle's body control module decides what voltage the alternator produces, and it changes constantly.

This matters because turn-counting controllers depend on consistent motor speed to maintain accurate position tracking. The motor in your antenna is a simple DC motor. Change the voltage, change the speed. Change the speed, and the time window in which the controller expects to see reed switch pulses shifts. The result is position drift — the controller thinks the antenna is at one position when it is actually at another. Over several band changes, the error accumulates.

💡 TIP

The fix is a regulated 13.8V DC power supply between the vehicle battery and the controller's motor drive output. This holds the motor voltage constant regardless of what the alternator is doing. SWR-seeking controllers are less affected by voltage variation since they do not depend on motor speed for position accuracy — they simply hunt for minimum SWR regardless of how fast the motor turns.

Homebrew Microcontroller Controllers

The Arduino, ESP32, and Raspberry Pi Pico platforms have made it practical for technically inclined operators to build their own antenna controllers. A homebrew controller can include features that no commercial unit offers: Bluetooth or WiFi control from a phone, OLED display showing frequency and position, CI-V decoding for automatic band tracking, SWR sensing with a built-in directional coupler, and whatever else you can write code for.

There is a growing community of documented designs, shared code, and published projects. If you are comfortable with a soldering iron and a code editor, the barrier to entry has never been lower.

🔑 KEY CONCEPT

The controller is the easy part. The choke is still the hard part. A beautifully engineered microcontroller with a color display and WiFi will behave just as badly as a commercial controller if the motor lead choke is inadequate or in the wrong location. Build and test the choke first. Get the RF environment right. Then build the controller. The fundamentals in this article apply to homebrew controllers exactly as they apply to commercial ones.

Radio Interface for Current Transceivers

Many current transceivers expose their CI-V or CAT control interface via USB rather than the traditional TTL-level serial port or DIN connector. This is fine for computer logging and digital modes, but it can complicate the connection to an antenna controller that expects a legacy serial input.

You may need a USB-to-serial adapter, a level converter, or a purpose-built interface cable to bridge between a modern radio's USB port and a controller's CI-V input. Check the controller documentation for the specific signal levels and protocol it expects.

⚠ WARNING

Digital mode software — FT8, JS8Call, WSJT-X, and similar applications — holds the radio's control port open for the duration of the session. If your antenna controller shares that same port, it will not be able to communicate with the radio while the digital software is running. Plan your port usage carefully. Use separate ports if your radio provides them, or implement a deliberate switching scheme. Running both on the same port simultaneously is a recipe for neither one working correctly.
