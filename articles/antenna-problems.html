Antenna Problems

Last updated: March 2026
Based on original work by Alan Applegate, K0BG Preserved & expanded for the amateur radio community
In This Article

Start With a Proper Mount

Before we discuss antenna problems, let us establish a baseline that will save you an enormous amount of grief: if your antenna is properly mounted — through-bolted or NMO-mounted to sheet metal with adequate ground plane beneath it — then no additional grounding, strapping, scraping, or supplemental connections are necessary. None. The vehicle body, properly bonded, is your ground plane, and it works exactly as intended without help.

I cannot overstate how much time and frustration this single fact will save you. If you find yourself reaching for copper braid, ground straps, or a wire brush to scrape paint, stop and ask yourself: is my antenna properly mounted? Nine times out of ten, the answer is no, and no amount of supplemental strapping will compensate for a fundamentally flawed mount.

🔑 KEY CONCEPT

Bonding is not a workaround for improper antenna mounting. If your antenna is not mounted correctly, bonding straps will not fix it. Fix the mount first. Then — and only then — address bonding if specific electrical measurements indicate it is needed.

The Ground Strap Problem

This section will be controversial, so let me be direct: running a ground strap from your antenna mount to the vehicle frame can actually exacerbate common mode currents, ground loops, and RFI. Yes, you read that correctly. The very thing most amateurs reach for first — a ground strap — can make the problem worse.

Here is why. A ground strap creates a parallel current path. RF current on the coax shield, which should transition cleanly to the vehicle body at the antenna mount, now has an alternative route through the strap to the frame. This creates a loop. Loops radiate. Loops pick up noise. Loops cause the very problems you were trying to solve.

🔥 MYTH BUSTED

If adding a ground strap fixed an RFI problem, something else was wrong — typically a poor antenna mount connection, a bad coax shield bond, or inadequate metal-to-metal contact at the mounting point. The strap did not fix the real problem; it masked it by providing an alternative current path. Find and fix the actual cause.

There is a simple rule regarding ground straps that has served well for decades: any ground strap longer than 10 inches is too long. At HF frequencies, a strap of any meaningful length has significant inductance. It is not the low-impedance path to ground that most people imagine. It is a piece of wire with reactance, and it behaves accordingly — which is to say, unpredictably and often counterproductively.

Coax Shield Continuity

The coax shield must be electrically consistent with the start of the ground plane. In plain language: where the coax connects to the antenna mount, the shield must make solid, low-impedance contact with the vehicle metal that serves as your ground plane. If there is paint, corrosion, a rubber gasket, or any other insulating material between the coax shield connection and the vehicle body, you have a problem.

This sounds elementary, and it is. Yet it is the single most common cause of antenna system problems in mobile installations. The coax connector is tight, the antenna mount looks solid, but there is a thin layer of paint or primer between the mount and the vehicle body that nobody scraped away during installation. The result is an antenna that seems to tune, sort of works, but generates RFI, produces common mode currents, and performs well below expectations.

Check this connection first. Check it with an ohmmeter. Check it before you do anything else. You will be amazed how often this is the entire problem.

Motor Lead Choking — The Most Critical and Most Neglected Step

If you run a screwdriver antenna — and a great many mobile HF operators do — this section may be the single most important thing you read on this entire website. Pay attention.

The motor leads on a screwdriver antenna operate above RF ground. The motor sits at the top of the antenna, surrounded by the coil and whip, immersed in the RF field. The wires running down from the motor to your controller inside the vehicle are not just DC power leads. At RF frequencies, they are part of the antenna system. They carry RF current into the vehicle, through the controller, and into every wire connected to it.

⚠ WARNING

The RF voltage on motor leads is not trivial. Depending on the antenna system and frequency, RF voltage on unchoked motor leads can range from 250 volts to over 1,000 volts peak. This is enough to damage or destroy controller electronics, cause RF burns if touched during transmission, and inject substantial interference into every wire connected to the controller. Treat unchoked motor leads as live RF conductors, because they are.

These motor leads must be choked. This is not optional. This is not a nice-to-have. This is an absolute requirement for a properly functioning screwdriver antenna installation.

⚠ WARNING

The ferrite beads supplied by most screwdriver antenna manufacturers are wholly inadequate for proper RF choking. They may reduce the problem slightly, but they do not solve it. Do not rely on manufacturer-supplied beads as your only RF choke on the motor leads.

Building a Proper Motor Lead Choke

The proper choke for screwdriver antenna motor leads consists of 13 turns of nylon-insulated #18 wire wound on a suitable ferrite core. This produces approximately 10 kilohms of impedance at 10 MHz — enough to effectively block RF from traveling down the motor leads and into the vehicle.

Let me address the shortcuts I know some of you are already contemplating:

  • 4 or 5 turns is not enough. I have seen this recommended in forums, in club newsletters, and even by people who should know better. The impedance produced by 4-5 turns is a fraction of what is needed. You might see a slight improvement in RFI, declare victory, and live with a station that performs well below its potential. Use 13 turns.
  • There are no substitutes. Do not use clip-on ferrites snapped over the motor leads. Do not use a random assortment of beads slid over the wire. Do not use a ferrite rod with wire wrapped around it. Build the choke correctly or do not bother.
  • There are no workarounds. None. You cannot compensate for unchoked motor leads by adding ground straps, changing coax, rerouting cables, or any other technique. The RF on those leads must be stopped at the source.
🔑 KEY CONCEPT

The motor lead choke must be installed outside the vehicle, as close to the antenna base as practical. All wire between the motor and the choke is part of the antenna — it is in the RF field and carrying RF current. The choke must be placed before the motor leads enter the vehicle. Installing the choke inside the vehicle, under the dashboard, or at the controller is ineffective because the RF has already entered the vehicle by that point.

The Isolated Power Fallacy

A recurring suggestion in online forums is to run a separate, dedicated power circuit for the transceiver, isolated from the vehicle electrical system, as a way to eliminate RFI and common mode problems. This does not work. Isolating the transceiver power does not eliminate RFI or common mode currents because those problems originate in the antenna system, not the power supply wiring.

What isolated power does do is reduce the voltage available to the antenna motor. The voltage drop across long, separate power leads — especially if undersized — means the motor may see significantly less than battery voltage. At a 10-amp load, which is typical for a screwdriver motor during tuning, battery voltage at the motor can drop below 11.5 volts. The motor slows considerably. Tuning takes longer. You sit there on frequency, waiting, while other stations wonder if you have fallen asleep.

💡 TIP

Always perform antenna setup and tuning with the engine running. The alternator maintains battery voltage at approximately 13.8 to 14.2 volts, which keeps the antenna motor operating at proper speed and ensures the electrical system is in its normal operating state.

Polyfuse Protection Issues

Many screwdriver antenna controllers incorporate Polyfuse (resettable fuse) protection for the motor circuit. These are temperature-sensitive devices — their trip point decreases as ambient temperature drops. In cold weather, the motor draws higher current due to thicker lubricant in the motor bearings, and the Polyfuse trip point is lower than normal. The result: the Polyfuse opens prematurely, the motor stops, and you are stuck on whatever frequency you happened to be tuning to.

If you experience intermittent motor stalling in cold weather that resolves after the vehicle warms up, the Polyfuse is the likely culprit. The solution is to replace the Polyfuse with an appropriately rated conventional fuse, or to relocate the Polyfuse to a warmer location inside the vehicle. Understand, however, that you are removing a protection device — size the replacement fuse correctly.

Interrupted-Thread Antenna Bodies

Some screwdriver antennas use an interrupted-thread design on the antenna body, where the whip screws into the top of the coil form using a coarse thread that allows it to be inserted quickly at several discrete positions. This is a convenience feature for manually removing and reinstalling the whip in low-clearance situations.

⚠ WARNING

Interrupted-thread antenna designs are incompatible with automatic antenna controllers. SWR-seeking and turn-counting controllers have no way to detect whether the whip has skipped to a different thread position. The controller's memory and tuning logic assume the antenna geometry is continuous. If the whip jumps positions, the controller operates with incorrect assumptions, produces wrong tune readings, and may drive the antenna to its physical limits. Use interrupted-thread antennas with manual control only.

Controller and Radio Compatibility

Not all screwdriver antenna controllers work with all transceivers. This is not obvious, and manufacturers do not always make it clear.

A specific example worth calling out: the Icom IC-706 and IC-7000 are both popular mobile radios, and they look similar from a feature standpoint. Their SWR ports, however, are electrically different. Controllers designed for the IC-706 are not necessarily compatible with the IC-7000. If you upgrade from one to the other and your SWR-seeking controller behaves erratically — hunting continuously, never locking, or driving to limits — check controller compatibility before assuming the antenna or controller is defective.

DC Motor Polarity — Trust but Verify

Here is a fact that will make you shake your head: at least three screwdriver antenna manufacturers use whatever motor wire colors they happen to have on hand during assembly. Red does not necessarily mean positive. Black does not necessarily mean negative. The motor will run in either direction depending on polarity, and if the polarity is reversed from what your controller expects, the antenna will tune in the wrong direction — going up when the controller commands down, and vice versa.

Check motor direction on the bench before you install the antenna on the vehicle. Apply power, observe which direction the contact moves, and label the wires. This takes two minutes and prevents hours of frustration.

💡 TIP

When using Anderson Power Pole connectors for motor leads, do not use the standard red and black housings. Reserve red/black for power connections. Use a different color pair — yellow/blue, for example — to prevent accidental cross-connection and to make motor leads visually distinct from power wiring.

Coax Selection — Bigger Is Not Better

I regularly encounter amateurs running RG-213, LMR-400, or even hardline coax in their mobile installations, convinced that larger coax means lower loss and better performance. In a base station installation with a 100-foot run, they would be correct. In a mobile installation with a 10-foot run, they are wasting money and making the installation harder for no meaningful benefit.

🔥 MYTH BUSTED

In a typical mobile installation with roughly 10 feet of coax, the difference in loss between RG-8X and RG-213 is a fraction of a dB — an amount that is completely unmeasurable on the air. Use RG-8X. It is easier to route, easier to terminate, and occupies less space. Save the large coax for your base station feed line.

WARNING: Solid Center Conductor Coax

Do not use any coax with a solid center conductor in a mobile installation. Solid conductors will fail due to vibration stress sooner or later — it is a matter of when, not if. Use only stranded-center coax. If your antenna kit came with RG-58/U with a solid center conductor, replace it before installation.

There is also a practical limit to the RG-213 argument from the standpoint of common mode choking. Duplicating the recommended 7-turn common mode choke using RG-213-sized coax would require 49 separate one-turn beads — approximately $250 worth of ferrite at current prices. RG-8X makes proper choking straightforward and affordable.

Screwdriver Antenna Maintenance

Never lubricate the coil of a screwdriver antenna. I know this seems counterintuitive — the contact rides on the coil, metal on metal, and lubrication would seem to reduce wear and improve contact. It does neither.

Lubricant on the coil attracts brake dust. Every vehicle on the road generates brake dust, and it finds its way onto every exposed surface. Mix brake dust with lubricant and you get an abrasive paste that accelerates wear rather than reducing it. Add moisture — rain, dew, road spray — and you get a conductive slurry that promotes arcing between coil turns. The result is pitting, burning, and eventual coil failure.

WD-40 is especially problematic. It is not a lubricant — it is a water displacement compound that leaves a sticky residue. That residue is a magnet for every contaminant in the environment. If someone has already applied WD-40 or other lubricant to your antenna coil, clean it thoroughly with a non-residue solvent and leave it dry.

Checking for Shorts and Opens

When a screwdriver antenna motor stops responding, the first step is simple: measure the resistance of the motor feed wires at the controller end. Disconnect the motor leads from the controller and measure with an ohmmeter.

KEY VALUES: Motor Resistance

Normal motor resistance measured across the leads at the controller end will typically be between 4Ω and 15Ω, depending on make and model. There should be no continuity between either lead and chassis ground. If the leads are open or shorted to the mast, you have a wiring problem that must be traced before transmitting.

  • Open circuit (infinite resistance): You have a break somewhere in the motor leads — a severed wire, a corroded connector, or a failed connection at the motor itself.
  • Short circuit (near zero ohms): The motor leads are shorted together somewhere along the run, or the motor itself has failed with a shorted winding.
  • Normal motor resistance (typically 4–15 ohms): The motor wiring is intact. The problem lies elsewhere — controller, Polyfuse, power supply, or the motor brushes are worn but not yet failed.
KEY VALUES: Stall Current

Running current: 250 to 350 milliamps (typical), may be higher on some models. Stall current: typically more than doubles to as high as 2 amps on most screwdrivers — and at least one model reaches 8 amps at stall. Controllers that draw power from the radio's accessory socket are limited to 1 amp total on Icom and Yaesu models. In most radios, a circuit trace will fail before the fuse opens. The Icom IC-706 uses a 4 amp ATC fuse; the IC-7000/7100 uses 5 amps; Yaesu models use a surface-mounted 3.5 amp fuse. Use a separate power circuit for the controller — do not use the transceiver accessory port.

This takes 30 seconds with a multimeter and eliminates the most common failure modes immediately. Yet I have watched amateurs disassemble entire installations, replace controllers, and rewire vehicles before performing this basic check. Troubleshoot systematically. Start with the simple tests.

SWR Threshold Setting for Automatic Controllers

Ground losses vary significantly from location to location. If you set your automatic controller's SWR threshold too low while parked on a steel-reinforced concrete driveway (which is an excellent ground plane), the controller may not detect an adequate SWR dip when you move to a location with worse ground conductivity, and it will continue to hunt past the set point.

KEY VALUE: SWR Threshold

Set the SWR threshold on automatic antenna controllers to no lower than 1.5:1, and occasionally slightly higher. This prevents the controller from hunting indefinitely at locations with higher ground losses. Even controllers with automatic SWR threshold settings can exhibit this behavior. If yours does, raise the threshold manually.

Parting Thoughts

Most antenna problems in mobile installations are not antenna problems at all. They are installation problems, mounting problems, and choking problems. The antenna itself — whether it is a screwdriver, a hamstick, a resonator, or a whip — is typically the least problematic component in the system. It is everything between the antenna and the transceiver, and everything between the antenna mount and the vehicle body, where problems originate.

Fix the mount. Choke the motor leads. Verify the coax shield connection. Check resistance before replacing components. Follow these steps and you will solve the vast majority of mobile antenna problems before they require anything more exotic.

Modern Antenna Options and Mounting

The fundamentals above have not changed, but the range of available hardware has expanded. A few developments are worth discussing.

Non-Motorized Alternatives

The motorized screwdriver antenna has been the workhorse of mobile HF for decades, and it remains an excellent choice. But it is not the only choice. Manually-adjusted coil antennas — designs that use a silver-plated coil with a sliding contact that you set by hand before operating — have gained a following, and for good reason.

A manually-adjusted antenna eliminates the motor, the motor leads, the motor lead choke, the Polyfuse, and the controller. That is a significant reduction in complexity and a significant reduction in potential failure points. The trade-off is obvious: you cannot change bands from the driver's seat. You get out of the vehicle, move the contact, get back in, and operate. For an operator who stays on one band per trip — which describes a great many POTA activators and casual mobile operators — this is a welcome simplification.

The physics are identical to a motorized screwdriver. The coil is still a loading coil. The whip is still a whip. The antenna still needs a proper mount, proper coax, and proper common-mode choking. Eliminating the motor does not eliminate the need for a correctly engineered installation. It just removes one set of problems from the equation.

Modern Mounting Solutions

Hitch-receiver mounts have become a popular option for heavy HF antennas on trucks and SUVs. A receiver-mounted antenna bracket allows you to place a full-size screwdriver or manually-adjusted antenna at the rear of the vehicle, supported by a structural member that can handle the weight and wind loading. Many designs offer quick-disconnect capability, so you can remove the antenna for garages, car washes, and low clearances without tools.

Stake-pocket mounts — brackets that drop into the stake pockets on truck bed rails — are another option. They can provide good ground plane contact to the vehicle body, assuming the stake pocket itself has clean metal-to-metal contact with the bed rail. On some trucks this is the case; on others, the pockets are painted, powder-coated, or lined with plastic, and you are back to the same bonding problems as any other mount.

⚠ WARNING

Any aftermarket antenna mount — hitch receiver, stake pocket, or otherwise — still requires electrical bonding verification. A mount that is mechanically solid is not necessarily electrically connected to the vehicle body. Measure it. A mount bolted to a powder-coated hitch receiver with rubber isolators between the components has zero RF ground plane connection regardless of how sturdy it feels. See the Bonding article.

Verifying Choke Impedance

For years, building a motor lead choke meant following the recipe — 13 turns on the correct core — and trusting that the result was adequate. You had no practical way to verify it without expensive laboratory equipment.

That has changed. Low-cost handheld vector network analyzers (VNAs) are now widely available, and they can measure the impedance of your motor lead choke directly. You can see both the resistive (R) and reactive (X) components across the entire HF spectrum, confirm that you have adequate choking impedance where you need it, and identify any frequencies where the choke may be insufficient.

💡 TIP

Measure, don't assume. A handheld VNA takes the guesswork out of choke verification. Wind your choke, measure it, and know — rather than hope — that it is doing its job. If the impedance is low at a frequency you care about, add turns or change the core material. The data is right there on the screen.
